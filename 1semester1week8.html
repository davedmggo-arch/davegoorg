Week 8: Interior Design - Blocking

You will establish the architectural scale of a room through "blocking," focusing on creating a layout that effectively guides the camera and actor.
Start Learning

About This Course

  • Construct a 3D architectural "shell" using real-world dimensions to ensure environmental scale accuracy.
  • Design an "acting space" by strategically placing furniture to support camera movement and character paths.
  • Apply composition fundamentals and camera focal lengths to establish the visual story of an interior room.

Key Concepts

Step 1: The Architectural Container

We begin by creating the "shell" of your room. This step ensures your scale is grounded in reality and your viewport is optimized for interior work.

The Box: Start with a Mesh Cube. Set the Location Z to 1.25m so the base sits on the ground plane. Use one of the following scale ranges for your three iterations:
  • Small: 3m x 3m x 2.5m
  • Medium: 4m x 5m x 2.5m
  • Large: 6m x 6m x 2.5m (If choosing Large, you must include a partitioned Walk-in Closet).
Backface Culling: To see inside your room, enter Edit Mode, select all faces, and Flip Normals (Alt + N). Then, go to the Material Properties tab, create a material named M_Room_Internal, and check the Backface Culling box under Settings.

Structural Separation: Select the floor face and press Y to separate it from the walls. Do the same for the ceiling. This allows you to hide the ceiling independently to work from a "top-down" perspective.

Naming: Each room object should be named SM_Room_Shell_Small (or Medium/Large) and placed in its own collection: Room_Layout_01, Room_Layout_02, or Room_Layout_03.

1. Creating the Room Container

We start with a single Cube that acts as the "bounds" of our world.
  • The Mesh: Press Shift + A > Mesh > Cube.
  • The Position: In the Sidebar (Press N), under the Item tab, set the Location Z to 1.25m and the Dimension Z to 2.5m. This ensures the base sits on the ground plane.
  • The Dimensions: Set the Scale based on the chosen room size: Small (3m x 3m), Medium (4m x 5m), or Large (6m x 6m).
  • Naming: In the Outliner, name the object SM_Room_Shell_Small (or Medium or Large). SM stands for Static Mesh.

2. Outliner Filters (The Selection Toggle)

Before we go further, we need to control what we can and cannot touch.

  • The Funnel Icon: At the top right of the Outliner, click the Filter (funnel) icon.

  • Enable Selectable: Click the Arrow icon (Selectable) to make it active in your Outliner list.

  • The Benefit: You will now see an arrow icon next to every object. Once your room is built, you can click that arrow to "lock" the room shell. This allows you to click and drag props inside the room without accidentally moving the walls.

View Gallery (5)

3. Enabling Interior Visibility

  • Flip Normals: Enter Edit Mode (Tab), select all faces (A), and press Alt + N > Flip.
  • Backface Culling: Go to the Material Properties tab, create a material named M_Room_Internal, and under Settings, check Backface Culling.
  • The Result: The walls are now "one-way mirrors"—invisible from the outside, but solid from the inside.

4. Organizing into Collections

Each room iteration needs its own "folder" in the Outliner to keep the instances separate.

  • Right-click in the Outliner > New Collection.

  • Create three collections with these exact names:

    1. Room_Layout_01

    2. Room_Layout_02

    3. Room_Layout_03

5. The "Walk-In Closet" (Large Room Only)

If we choose the Large Room, we must add an internal wall.

  • In Edit Mode of the SM_Room_Shell_Large, add a new Cube.

  • Scale it to be a thin "Wall" (e.g., 0.1m thick) and position it to partition a corner of the room.

  • Name this mesh data Internal_Closet_Wall.

View Gallery (2)

6. Separating the Planes

To finalize the shell, we need to un-stitch the floor and ceiling.

  • Select the bottom face (floor) and press Y to Separate.

  • Select the top face (ceiling) and press Y to Separate.

  • This allows you to toggle the visibility of the ceiling while keeping it part of the same architectural object.

View Gallery (5)

7. Creating Holes for Windows and Doors

We will manually "cut" the holes into our geometry.

  • Loop Cuts: In Edit Mode, press Ctrl + R to add two vertical loop cuts for the width of the door/window, and two horizontal loop cuts for the height.

  • Delete Faces: Select the face where the opening should be and press X > Delete Faces.

Step 2: Scene Organization & The Workshop

    A professional workspace is a clean workspace. We will move our room layouts away from the center to make room for our "Master Assets."

    • Parking the Rooms: Move your three room shells along the X-axis (e.g., -10m, 0m, and 10m) so they don't overlap.

    • The World Origin: Keep the center of your grid (0,0,0) empty. This is your "Workshop" area where you will build the original versions of your furniture.

    • Visibility: Use this empty center space to focus on one prop at a time without the walls of the room getting in your way.

Step 3: Master Assets & Collection Instancing

Instead of copying and pasting objects, we will use Collection Instances. This means if you edit the "Master" version of your chair at the origin, every chair in every room layout updates automatically.

  • Master Collections: Create a new collection for each of the 8 required props: C_Bed, C_Desk, C_Chair, C_Door, C_Cabinet, C_Window, C_Lights, and C_Computer.

  • Building the "DNA": Build each prop at the World Origin (0,0,0) using identifiable blocking shapes:

    • Bed: Base cube plus a "Pillow" cube.

    • Computer: A vertical "Tower," a thin "Monitor," and a flat "Keyboard."

    • Chair: Seat, backrest, and a central stem.

    • Door/Window: Thin planes or cubes representing the frame and glass.

  • Instancing: Inside your Room Layout collections, press Shift + A > Collection Instance to bring in a "ghost" of your master props. Move and rotate these instances to design your room.

The Workflow: "Build Once, Place Everywhere"

Before modeling, students must understand that they are creating Master Templates. They will build the object at the center of the grid, and then place "ghost" versions (Instances) into their actual room layouts.

1. Creating the Master Collections

In the Outliner, right-click and select New Collection for every required prop. It is vital that these collections are named clearly so they are easy to find when instancing.

  • C_Bed

  • C_Desk

2. Distinguishable Blocking for Each Prop

Students should not just use a single cube. They must use a few basic shapes to make the prop recognizable. Here is how they should block out the 8 required items:

  • The Bed: Start with a large base cube scaled to roughly $1.5m \times 2m$. Add a smaller, flatter cube at one end to represent a Pillow.

  • The Desk: Create a thin, wide cube for the Desktop and four long, thin cubes for the Legs.

  • The Chair: Use one cube for the Seat, a vertical cube for the Backrest, and a single cylinder or cube for the Base/Stem.

  • The Door: Since we aren't using Booleans, create a thin cube ($0.05m$ thick) scaled to $0.9m \times 2.1m$. This represents the "Door Leaf" that will be placed against the wall.

  • The Cabinet: A tall, deep rectangle. Add two thin vertical cubes on the front to represent Cabinet Doors/Handles.

  • The Window: A flat, thin cube. Use the Loop Cut tool (Ctrl + R) to add a crosshair in the middle, then Inset (I) the faces to create a basic frame.

  • The Lights: For a ceiling light, use a simple Cylinder scaled thin. For a desk lamp, use a small cube base and a sphere for the bulb/shade.

  • The Computer: This is the "Hero" prop. Use a vertical rectangle for the PC Tower, a very thin wide rectangle for the Monitor, and a flat rectangle on the desk for the Keyboard.

3. The Instancing Process

Once a prop is blocked out in its collection, it’s time to move it into the 3 rooms.

  • Click outside of your Master Collections into your Room_Layout_01 collection.

  • Press Shift + A > Collection Instance > Select the prop (e.g., C_Bed).

  • An "Instance" will appear at the origin. Move, rotate, and place this instance into your room shell.

  • Repeat this for all 8 props across all 3 room layouts.

Step 4: Layout Iteration & Pitch

    Now you play the role of an interior designer. You must prove that your room is a functional "Acting Space."

    • The 3 Variations: Create three distinct layouts. Because we are working with 3 different rooms, we need to meet the style of the room with its size.

    • Collision Check: Ensure there is enough "walking space." Can your character (from the previous project) walk from the door to the bed without clipping through a cabinet?

    • The Render: Use the Eevee engine. Position your camera outside the room (utilizing your backface culling) to get a clear "dollhouse" view of the layout.

    • Naming Convention: You must submit three PNG images using this exact format:

1. Compositional Variety

We should not simply move the bed a few inches to the left for each version. Each of the three layouts should serve a different "lifestyle" or "narrative" to give the instructor a real choice.

  • Option A: The Work-from-Home Setup. Prioritize the desk and computer near the window for natural light, placing the bed in a more secluded area.

  • Option B: The Relaxation Suite. Place the bed as the focal point in the center of the room, with the desk tucked away or used as a nightstand.

  • Option C: The "Lived-in" Compact Room. Use the small room footprint to show how all 8 props can fit efficiently without feeling claustrophobic (ensuring there is still "walking space" for the character).

2. Checking the "Acting Space"

Before taking the final screenshots, students must perform a "Collision Check." Even though these are just blocks, the spatial logic must hold up:

  • Clearance: Ensure the Chair instance has enough room to be pulled out from the Desk without hitting the Bed.

  • Access: The Door instance should not be blocked by the Cabinet.

  • Ergonomics: The Computer monitor should be at a logical height relative to the Chair.

  • The Character Test: If the student still has their character model from the previous week, they should move it into the layout to ensure the Window isn't at the character's knees and the Ceiling Light isn't hitting them in the head.

3. Setting up the Submission View

Since we are using Eevee and Backface Culling, students can easily take "Dollhouse" style photos.

  • Camera Placement: Position the viewport camera outside the room shell looking in. Because of the backface culling set up in Step 1, the "back" walls will disappear, giving a clear view of the furniture arrangement.

  • Viewport Shading: Set the Viewport to Material Preview or Rendered mode so the instructor can see the distinction between the objects clearly.

  • Angle: Use a "3/4 Isometric" view (looking down from a corner) to capture the floor plan and the height of the props simultaneously.

4. Final Export & Naming

Each student must export three separate images. They should use the View > Viewport Render Image command in Blender for a clean shot without the grid lines or 3D gizmos.
