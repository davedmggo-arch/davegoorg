Week 2: Detailing Assets and Procedural Material Systems

This week, we move from the abstract block-out phase into high-fidelity asset development. You will replace your primitive shapes with finalized 3D models and build "Smart Materials" that adapt to your environment's geometry. The focus is on modularity: creating a small number of assets that can be reused effectively across your tile.

Start Learning!!

Key Concepts

1 Data Unification: Merging Color and Weight

In traditional 3D workflows, artists often have to perform the same task twice. You might paint a Weight Map to tell the computer where to place grass and then switch to a different mode to paint a Color Attribute to tell the computer that the grass should be green. In modern visual development, we unify these into a single Source of Truth.

This approach ensures that if you paint a specific area red, the computer automatically understands that this area should be physically flat and visually colored like dirt. We achieve this by using a simple Geometry Nodes bridge to move data between different systems.

  • The Artistic Input: Paint your visual layout using Color Attributes. For example, use white to represent where you want detail and black for where you want the ground to stay plain.
  • The Logic Bridge: Use a Named Attribute Node inside Geometry Nodes and select your color map.
  • The Data Storage: Use a Store Named Attribute Node. Set this to Float and Point.
  • The Connection: Connect the color data to this node and give it a generic example name like ATTR_Logic_Map.
  • The Result: You can now call this single attribute name in both your Displacement Modifier and your Shader Editor. One map now controls both the physical height of the ground and the visual texture.

2 Procedural Water and Foam Logic

Before building the variations of the environment, you must establish the water system. Water in stylized environments needs interactive details, such as foam where the liquid meets the shore, to feel integrated into the world.

1. The Ocean Base

Start with a high-density plane and add an Ocean Modifier. To manually paint foam, you must Apply the modifier. This converts the mathematical waves into actual geometry (vertices) that can store your paint data. Use a generic naming convention for this object, such as SM_Water_Primary.

2. Painting Foam Attributes

Enter Vertex Paint mode and create a new attribute. Paint white streaks along the edges where the water would naturally hit the land. In the Shader Editor, use an Attribute Node to pull this data. This acts as a mask to mix your deep water material with a bright, opaque foam material.

3. Raising Foam Geometry

To make the foam look thick and "chunky" rather than like a flat sticker, add a Displacement Modifier to the water plane.

  • Set the modifier input to Attribute instead of a texture.
  • Type in the example name of your foam attribute, such as ATTR_Foam_Mask.
  • The modifier will now physically lift the white painted areas above the base water level, creating volume that catches highlights and shadows.

3 Creating Tile Variations

With the master logic finished, you can generate the specific tiles needed for the IP development package. By duplicating the setup and simply repainting the attributes, you can create new environments in seconds.

1. The Path Tile

This tile establishes the scale of the forest. You paint a linear attribute through the center. Because of the logic established in section 2.1, the ground will automatically dip to form a smooth dirt trail while the displacement pushes the surrounding grass areas upward.

2. The Target Tile

A Target is a point of interest, such as an ancient ruin or a hero tree. Duplicate your path tile and use a naming convention like SM_Tile_Target_A. Clear the linear paint and create a circular focal point in the middle. The procedural assets will automatically move to the perimeter, framing the center of the tile.

3. The Non-Interactive Tile

This tile serves as a boundary to tell the player they cannot pass. Duplicate the tile again and name it something like SM_Tile_NonInteractive_01. Bring in the water logic from section 2.2. Paint the ground attribute so the water material is dominant, and use the foam displacement to make the river look deep and impassable.

4 Procedural Rocks: Fractured Stone and Moss

Rocks serve as the skeletal structure of an environment. In visual development, we want them to appear heavy, angular, and naturally integrated into the terrain. By using procedural modifiers, we can create complex geological shapes from very simple starting points.

1. Generating the Base Geometry

Start by adding a Cube to the scene. Add a Subdivision Surface modifier set to at least level 3. This creates a rounded "quad-sphere" which provides a much more even distribution of vertices than a standard UV sphere, preventing texture stretching at the top and bottom of the rock.

2. Fracturing with Voronoi Displacement

To give the rock its sharp, stony character, add a Displacement Modifier. Create a new texture and set the type to Voronoi. Inside the texture settings, change the Distance Metric to Distance Squared. This creates sharp, angular cracks and flat planes that mimic fractured granite or limestone. Adjust the strength of the modifier to control how aggressive the rock features appear.

3. Painting Moss and Surface Detail

Before painting moss, you must Apply the Subdivision Surface modifier. This converts the virtual surface into real vertices, providing the necessary density for high-quality painting. Use a Color Attribute to paint the top-facing surfaces where moss would naturally grow. In the Shader Editor, use an Attribute Node to blend a stone material with a moss material based on this paint.

5 Simple Stylized Shrubs

Shrubs provide the softness and fullness needed to break up the hard edges of the ground and rocks. The goal is to create "puffy" clusters that read well from a distance, which is essential for defining the visual language of a new IP.

1. The Leaf Cluster Base

Start with a Cube and add a Subdivision Surface modifier. This creates a soft, rounded clump that serves as the volume for a cluster of leaves. This method is much more efficient for computer memory than modeling individual leaves.

2. Displacing for Volume

Add a Displacement Modifier set to Clouds. Keep the scale of the clouds large to create broad, lumpy shapes that mimic the silhouette of a dense bush. Once you are satisfied with the general silhouette, Apply the subdivision modifier to finalize the geometry for painting.

3. Stylized Color Grading

Create a Color Attribute and paint darker tones at the bottom of the shrub and lighter, warmer greens at the top. This simulates "ambient occlusion" and sunlight directly on the model, ensuring the asset looks high-quality regardless of the complex lighting in the final scene.

6 Asset Assembly: Variance and Composition

The final step is to populate the Path, Target, and Non-Interactive tiles. To make an environment look professional, you must avoid the "grid look" where objects appear repeated or artificial.

  • Variance in Scale: Never leave assets at their default size. Create at least three variations of every rock and shrub: small, medium, and large. A cluster of three different-sized rocks looks like a natural formation, whereas three identical rocks look like a digital error.
  • Random Rotation: Rotate every asset randomly on the vertical Z-axis. Because our displacement modifiers are procedural, spinning a rock 90 degrees reveals a completely different set of fractures, allowing you to reuse the same model dozens of times without the viewer noticing.
  • Compositional Framing: Place assets with purpose. On the Path tile, use shrubs to guide the eye forward. On the Target tile, use large rocks to "frame" the central object. On the Non-Interactive tile, place assets densely to create a visual wall that signals the area is blocked.
  • Organization: Ensure all assets follow a consistent naming convention. For example, use SM_Rock_A for meshes and COL_Props for your outliner collections. This allows for a clean production pipeline and easy hand-offs to other departments.
