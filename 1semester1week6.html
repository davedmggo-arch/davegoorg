Week 6: Basic Character: Materials & Animation

You will transform your model into a living character by applying custom textures and recording a short performance through keyframing.

Start Learning

Learning Outcomes

  • Perform normal map baking to transfer high-resolution sculptural details onto a low-poly production mesh.
  • Create and apply custom textures using a combination of hand-painting and image-based mapping.
  • Execute a basic character performance using keyframes and weight painting to ensure smooth movement.

Key Concepts

1. Custom Textures & Map Baking

This week, you will transform your gray model into a finished character by "baking" the details of your high-poly sculpt into the textures of your low-poly mesh.

  • Normal Map Baking: This process captures the wrinkles, pores, and muscle definitions from your Week 4 sculpt and projects them onto the low-poly mesh. This creates the illusion of high detail without the heavy computer processing cost.
  • Hand-Painted vs. Image Textures: You can choose to paint colors directly onto the 3D model in "Texture Paint" mode or use existing image files to define the look of skin, fabric, or hair.
  • The Shader Setup: You will use the Principled BSDF once again to manage how shiny (Roughness) or dull different parts of the character appear.

2. Animation Test: The "Performance"

Since your character is now rigged, it is time to bring it to life through keyframing.

  • Keyframing: Animation is the record of change over time. You will set "Keyframes" (poses) at specific points on the timeline, and Blender will calculate the movement between them.
  • The Head Turn: For this project, you will animate a simple head turn, blink, or slight expression change. This serves as a test to ensure your rig is functional.
  • Weight Painting Refinement: If you notice the mesh "collapsing" or stretching strangely during the animation, you will return to Weight Paint mode to adjust how much each bone influences the surrounding vertices.

3. Rendering the Character

To showcase your character, you will apply the lighting principles learned in Week 3 to create a final video clip.

  • Consistency: Use the 3-point lighting setup (Key, Fill, and Rim) to ensure the character stands out from the background.
  • Texture Resolution: Ensure your textures are at least 2K (2048x2048) resolution so the character remains crisp even when the camera is close to the face.
  • Motion Blur: You may enable Motion Blur in the render settings to give the movement a more natural, filmic quality.

4. Essential Shortcuts & Tools

  • Set Keyframe: Hover over the 3D viewport or a property and press I.
  • Play/Pause Animation: Press Spacebar.
  • Toggle Pose Mode: With the Rig selected, press Ctrl + Tab to begin posing.
  • Texture Paint Mode: Press Ctrl + Tab and select Texture Paint to paint directly on the mesh.
  • Reset Pose: Press Alt + R to reset rotation or Alt + G to reset position of a selected bone.

5. Best Practices & Pitfalls

  • Avoid "Popping": Ensure your movement isn't too fast between two keyframes, which can cause the mesh to "pop" or jitter.
  • Subtle Movement: When animating a head turn, remember that the eyes usually move slightly before the head does. This adds a sense of "life" to the character.
  • Check Normals: If your texture looks black or broken in certain spots, check your face orientation (Normals) before baking your maps.
