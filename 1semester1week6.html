Week 6: Advanced Character Components & Technical Workflows

Following the block out of your character’s head and torso, we now branch into the development of the full character bust. This phase focuses on constructive modeling for limbs, stylistic hair, and the technical workflows used by professional studios to prepare models for animation.
Start Learning

Key Concepts

Essential Technical Foundations

Before you begin adding new components, you must ensure your object data is clean and your origin points are placed with precision. Proper setup prevents your sculpt brushes from feeling oval or distorted.


1. Applying Transforms

  • How to Apply: In Object Mode, select your objects and press Ctrl + A then select Rotation and Scale.
  • Why it matters: This resets the internal math of the object so that a 10cm brush stroke is actually 10cm on every axis.
Pro-Tip: If your Symmetry (X-Mirror) feels "off center" even though the model looks centered, it is almost always because the Rotation or Scale hasn't been applied.

2. Origin Points and Control

The Origin Point (the small orange dot) is the pivot for all movement, rotation, and scaling. For character components, placing this accurately is vital for modifiers like Mirror to work correctly.

  • Setting the Origin: Right-click an object and navigate to Set Origin. You can choose "Origin to Geometry" to center it or "Origin to 3D Cursor" for custom placement.
  • Precision Control: Use Shift + S to snap the 3D Cursor to a specific vertex or the world center (0,0,0) before setting your origin to that cursor.
  • Pivot Point: Your origin determines how an object rotates; for example, an eyeball's origin must be at its exact geometric center to rotate naturally in the socket.
Pro-Tip: Always keep your character's main body origin at the world center (0,0,0). This acts as the "anchor" for every other accessory you create.

1. Constructive Appendages: Arms and Hands

  • Arm Blocking: Use cylinders or elongated spheres to define upper arm and forearm on one side of your character. Position these in a standard A-pose to ensure shoulders can deform naturally during animation.
  • Mitten Method: Start with a squashed cube for palm. Simplifying hand into a solid mitten shape is an excellent way to maintain a strong graphic silhouette while keeping topology manageable.
  • Fusing Limbs: Once primitives are positioned and you are happy with the silhouette, press Ctrl + J to Join them and Ctrl + R to Voxel Remesh connection points.
  • Mirror Modifier: After fusing arm to torso, add Mirror Modifier to character. Use Eyedropper tool to select Body/Torso as Mirror Object. This ensures fused arm mirrors perfectly across world center.
Pro-Tip: When modeling A-pose, keep a slight bend in elbow. This "pre-bend" helps rigger later by showing computer exactly which way arm is supposed to fold.

2: Standard Character Accessories: Hair, Eyes, and Lashes

  • Eyeballs: Add UV Sphere but do not fuse it to head mesh. Keeping eyes as separate objects allows them to rotate during animation.
  • Face Project for Brows and Lashes: Start with simple Plane. Enable snapping, set snapping type to Face Project, and ensure Project Individual Elements is checked. This allows you to move vertices directly onto surface of your sculpt.
  • Extrusion Safety Rule: You must turn Face Project snapping OFF before you extrude (E) brows or lashes outward. If you leave it on, snapping logic will fight you and lock new faces back into mesh.
  • Subdivided Cube Hair: Instead of using sculpt brushes to pull out strands, start with Subdivided Cube (Create a cube and press Ctrl + 2 to create a Subdivision Modifier with 2 Levels). Use Loop Cuts (Ctrl + R in Edit Mode) to add segments and move vertices to form individual clumps or strands. This provides superior control over topology and maintains clean quads.
  • Quad Rule: Always model using four sided faces (Quads). Avoid triangles or N-gons. Keeping faces as quads is an industry standard habit that ensures models can be subdivided and animated without visual glitches.
Pro-Tip: For graphic hair, follow "Large, Medium, Small" rule. Create one or two large primary masses, three or four medium clumps, and a few small "stray" strands to create visual interest and rhythm.

3. Clothing and Hard Surface Components

Clothing is modeled over the character's body using a combination of snapping tools and modifiers to achieve a natural, weighted look. For extra details like buttons or belt buckles, you will shift from sculpting into hard surface modeling to maintain crisp edges.

  • Base Creation: Use Face Project snapping to draw your base clothing geometry directly onto the character's skin. Ensure you are working in Quads to maintain proper topology habits for future deformation.
  • The Solidify Modifier: Once your base mesh is snapped, it will look like it is painted onto the skin. To fix this, add a Solidify Modifier in the modifier panel. This automatically generates a "shell" around your 2D faces to give them real, physical thickness.
  • Refining Volume: After adding thickness, use a combination of Scaling in Edit Mode and Inflating in Sculpt Mode to push the fabric away from the body. Use the Grab brush to "nudge" the fabric into place, ensuring it sits naturally over the character's form rather than clipping into the skin.
  • Hard Surface Accessories: For items like buttons, zippers, or buckles, do not sculpt them. Instead, create these as separate components in Object Mode. Add a primitive like a Cube or Cylinder and use Edit Mode to extrude and bevel the edges for a clean, manufactured look.
  • X-Ray Mode: Press Alt + Z to toggle X-Ray mode. This is essential when creating clothing so you can see through the mesh and select vertices on both the front and back of the garment.
Pro-Tip: Gravity and tension are your best friends for realism. Clothing folds should originate from tension points, such as where fabric is pulled tight at the armpits or buttoned at the waist. Even without the use of sculpting brushes, you can achieve these "stress lines" by nudging vertices with the Grab brush to follow the direction of the tension.

Method 1: Creating Clothing With Primitives

This method is perfect for tubular items such as sleeves, gauntlets, or leggings. It is fast and ensures your clothing follows the natural volume of the limbs.


Step 1: Starting Lean with Primitives

Press (Shift + A) and select a Cylinder or UV Sphere. In the Add menu at the bottom left, set your vertices to 8 or 12. Working with low polygon counts makes the initial shaping much easier to manage. If the primitive has extra horizontal rings you do not need, select the edge loop with (Alt + Left Click), press X, and choose Dissolve Edges.


Step 2: Aligning to the Model Sheet

In Edit Mode, rotate and scale your primitive until it matches the angle of the limb on your front model sheet. Use Loop Cuts (Ctrl + R) to add geometry only where the silhouette changes, such as the bulge of a muscle or a taper at the wrist. After placing a cut, hit S to scale it immediately to fit the sheet.


Step 3: Designing for Proper Deformation

It is vital that your loops are clean circles around the limbs. Avoid spiral topology, as this will cause the mesh to collapse during animation. Ensure you have at least three loop cuts at bending points like elbows or knees. This allows the mesh to accordion properly when the character moves.


Step 4: Refining with Bevels

To give your primitive a manufactured look, select the top and bottom rims of the gear and press (Ctrl + B). This adds a Bevel, creating a small angled face that catches the light and makes the object look like a solid piece of hard surface equipment.

Primitive Control and Transform Spaces

When creating clothing, the initial generation of your geometry is critical. Start by adding a Cylinder (Shift + A). Before moving it, locate the Add Cylinder menu in the bottom-left of the viewport. Set the Vertices to 8 or 12. This low resolution is essential for maintaining control during the "block-out" phase.


The N-Panel and Item Transforms

Press N to open the Sidebar and select the Item tab.


  • Scale Management: If you resize your cylinder in Object Mode, your Scale values will change from 1.0. This can cause unpredictable results with modifiers like Solidify. Always press Ctrl + A and select Scale to reset these values to 1.0 after your initial sizing.

  • Dimensions: Use the Dimensions fields to ensure your garment matches the real-world scale of your character.


Global vs. Local Transformations

You can toggle your Transformation Orientation at the top center of the viewport.

  • Global: Best for general placement along the world's grid.

  • Local: Essential when a limb is at an angle. Switching to Local allows you to move the cylinder precisely along the axis of the arm or leg rather than fighting the world's X, Y, and Z axes.

Advanced Scaling and Snapping

A perfect cylinder does not fit a human body. You must adjust the proportions to match the "oval" nature of the torso and limbs.


X and Y Scaling in Edit Mode

Select an edge loop (Alt + Left Click) and use axis-specific scaling to match the body's thickness:

  • S then X: Widens the garment to match the broadness of the chest or shoulders.

  • S then Y: Adjusts the depth, pulling the fabric closer to the spine or sternum.


Face Project and Face Nearest

To make your geometry "hug" the skin without using a modifier, enable the Magnet icon (Snapping) at the top of the viewport.

  • Set Snap To to Face Project.

  • Check Project Individual Elements.

  • When you move vertices or loops, they will now "stick" to the surface of the character's skin, ensuring a perfect fit.

Constructing a Shirt!

Instead of building separate parts, we will grow the shirt from a single cylinder to ensure a continuous, clean mesh.


Step 1: The Torso and Shoulders

Position your cylinder around the torso and use Loop Cuts (Ctrl + R) to define the waist and chest.

  • Shoulders: Select the top edge loop. Press E to extrude and S to scale outward, covering the tops of the shoulders.

  • Neckline: Delete the center-top faces to create a hole for the neck.


Step 2: Creating Sleeve Openings

You need a "socket" for the arms. Ensure you have vertical loop cuts running down the sides of the torso.

  • Select the faces on the side of the chest where the arm emerges and delete them.

  • This creates an open edge loop.


Step 3: Extruding and Bridging

  • Extruding: Select the new armhole loop and press E to pull the sleeve down the arm.

  • Bridging: If you have a separate sleeve cylinder, select both the armhole loop and the sleeve loop, right-click, and choose Bridge Edge Loops. This connects the two pieces with new faces instantly.

  • Creating Faces: For small gaps, select two edges and press F to create a single face manually.

Joining, Separating, and Sculpting

Managing Objects

  • Join (Ctrl + J): If you modeled sleeves and the torso as separate objects, select them and press Ctrl + J to merge them into one.

  • Separate (P): If you need to break a piece off (like a collar), select the faces in Edit Mode and press P > Selection.


Organic Shaping in Sculpt Mode

Switch to Sculpt Mode with a Mirror Modifier active.

  • The Grab Brush (G): This is your primary tool for "tucking" the shirt. Remember that the brush moves geometry perpendicular to your camera. If you want to pull a shirt forward away from the chest, you must be in Side View (Numpad 3).

  • Mirroring: Use the X-Symmetry toggle at the top right to ensure your sculpt affects both sides of the shirt equally.

Thickness and Smoothing

To complete the hard-surface look, apply your modifier stack in this specific order:

  1. Mirror: For perfect symmetry.

  2. Solidify: To give the shirt physical thickness (e.g., 0.02m). Use an Offset of 1.0 to grow the fabric outward.

  3. Subdivision Surface (Ctrl + 2): To smooth the low-poly quads. If the edges look too round, add Support Loops (Ctrl + R) very close to the hems to sharpen them.

Method 2: Creating Clothing With Planes

This method is fantastic for high-precision pieces like breastplates, shoulder pads, or tactical vests. We will build the clothing from the front, wrap it to the back, and then bridge the gap.


Step 1: Tracing the Front Silhouette

In Object Mode, press (Shift + A) and add a Plane. Rotate it 90 degrees on the X-axis so it faces you. In Edit Mode, move your vertices to match the front-view model sheet. Use Extrude (E) to build out the flat panel of the garment. Remember: Always work with Quads (four-sided faces) to ensure the mesh deforms smoothly later!


Step 2: Creating the Backside

Once your front panel is complete, follow these steps to create the back:

  • Duplicate: Select all geometry with the A key and press (Shift + D).
  • Position: Move the duplicate along the Y-axis until it sits behind your character model, aligned with your back model sheet.
  • Flip Normals: Because we duplicated the front, the back faces are pointing the wrong way. Press (Alt + N) and select Flip so the outside of the mesh faces away from the body.

Step 3: Bridging the Seams

Now we need to connect the two pieces to create a solid object. Select the edge loops on the sides of the front and back panels where a side seam would naturally occur. Right-click and choose Bridge Edge Loops. This creates the side panels of your clothing instantly.


Step 4: Shaping and Sculpting

Switch to the Side View (Numpad 3). Use Loop Cuts (Ctrl + R) to add horizontal detail. This allows you to pull the middle of the mesh forward or backward to match the depth of your character. Finally, switch to Sculpt Mode and use the Grab Brush to subtly nudge the armor so it fits snugly against the base model without clipping.

Transform Control and Axis Alignment

For high-precision work, we start with a Plane (Shift + A). Before editing, you must ensure your object’s mathematical data is aligned with the world's coordinate system. This prevents tools like the Mirror modifier or Bevel from behaving erratically.


The N-Panel and Applying Transforms

Press N to open the Sidebar and select the Item tab.

  • The Goal: For predictable modeling, your Rotation should be $0^{\circ}$ on all axes, and your Scale should be 1.0.

  • The Process: If you rotated your plane $90^{\circ}$ to face the camera or scaled it to fit the torso, those numbers are no longer "neutral." Press Ctrl + A and select All Transforms.

  • Why it matters: Applying transforms "freezes" the current state of the mesh as its new default. This ensures that the object’s Local axis perfectly matches the Global axis, meaning "Up" is always Z and "Forward" is always Y.

The Plane-to-Volume "Wrap"

This method ensures perfect control over the silhouette before committing to 3D volume.

Step 1: Tracing the Silhouette with Extrusions and Loops

In Edit Mode, we will build the front panel of the garment.

  • Extrude (E): Select an edge and pull it out to follow the major shapes of the clothing.

  • Loop Cuts (Ctrl + R): Once you have a basic shape, use loop cuts to add vertical or horizontal subdivisions. This allows you to "bend" the mesh later to fit the curvature of the chest.

  • Quad Discipline: Ensure every face remains a four-sided Quad. This is critical for clean deformation and for ensuring your loop cuts travel predictably around the mesh.


Step 2: Creating the Backside

Once the front panel is complete, we create the back of the garment using a duplicate.

  • Duplicate: Select all geometry (A) and press Shift + D.

  • Position: Move the duplicate along the Y-axis until it sits behind the character model.

  • Flip Normals: Because the back is a duplicate of the front, the "outside" faces are likely pointing inward toward the character's spine. Press Alt + N and select Flip so the normals face outward.


Step 3: Bridging the Seams

To turn these two flat panels into a single, water-tight object, we must connect them at the sides and shoulders.

  • Bridge Edge Loops: Select the corresponding open edge loops on the sides of the front and back panels. Right-click and choose Bridge Edge Loops. This automatically generates the "side panels" of the garment.

  • Manual Faces (F): For smaller gaps or specific connections, select two parallel edges and press F to create a single face manually.

Anatomical Fitting: Scaling and Snapping

Now that the garment is a solid volume, we must adjust it to "hug" the character's anatomy.


X and Y Scaling in Edit Mode

Select an edge loop (Alt + Left Click) and use axis-specific scaling to match the body's thickness. Because we Applied Transforms in Step 1, these axes will behave exactly as expected.

  • S then X: Widens the garment to match the broadness of the chest.

  • S then Y: Adjusts the depth, pulling the panels closer to the ribs or back.


Face Project Snapping

To make your geometry stick to the skin, enable the Magnet icon (Snapping) at the top of the viewport.

  • Set Snap To to Face Project and check Project Individual Elements.

  • When you move vertices, they will "stick" to the surface of the character's mesh, ensuring a perfect fit without manual guesswork.

Refinement: Sculpting and Joining

Organic Shaping in Sculpt Mode

Switch to Sculpt Mode with a Mirror Modifier active to maintain symmetry.

  • The Grab Brush (G): This is your primary tool for "tucking" the garment. Remember that the brush moves geometry perpendicular to your camera. If you want to pull a vest forward away from the chest, you must rotate your camera to the Side View (Numpad 3) before grabbing.

5. The Final Finish: The Modifier Stack

The last step is to add physical weight and smoothness to the low-poly block-out. Apply these modifiers in this exact order:

  1. Mirror: Stays at the top to ensure symmetry while you continue to tweak the model.

  2. Solidify: This gives the garment physical thickness (e.g., 0.02m). Set the Offset to 1.0 to grow the fabric outward so it does not clip into the character's skin.

  3. Subdivision Surface (Ctrl + 2): This smooths the quads into a high-fidelity finish. If the edges look too round, add Support Loops (Ctrl + R) in Edit Mode very close to the edges of the garment to keep them crisp.

4. Technical Workflow: Applied Retopology and Mesh Health

  • Targeted Retopology: In this project, you will not be retopologizing head. You will focus retopology efforts solely on clothing and accessories to learn fundamentals of edge flow.
  • Edge Flow and Quads: Ensure new mesh consists entirely of four sided faces. Clean edge loops around openings of clothes (like sleeves or collars) are vital for model to behave correctly in an animation pipeline.
  • Learning Resources: If you wish to learn advanced head retopology, additional material is available on a separate page for independent study.
  • X-Ray Mode: Press Alt + Z to toggle X-Ray mode. This is essential when creating clothing so you can see through mesh and select vertices on both front and back of garment.
Pro-Tip: Keep polygon count as low as possible during retopology. It is significantly easier to adjust 20 vertices to find a perfect curve than it is to manage 200. You can always add more detail later with Subdivision Surface modifier.
