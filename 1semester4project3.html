Project 3: Advanced Prop Design (Weeks 9-12)

The semester culminates in an expansive exterior environment. This project focuses on Cinematic Composition and the integration of all previous semester elements into a unified, professional-grade visual narrative.

Start Learning!!

The Lore: Smyder and The Claws

Smyder is a clever, energetic boy who has decided that Adventure Island would be much better if he were in charge. Dressed in a weathered captain's coat with deep pockets full of treats, he is never seen without his "band of cats." These are not ordinary pets; they are a coordinated unit of feline mischief-makers that follow his every command.


Smyder doesn't want to hurt anyone, but he does want to make the island a bit more chaotic. He needs specialized gear to help him and his cats pull off their nefarious plans.

Project Details

Prop Selection Summary

  • The Static-Claw Gauntlet (Hands): A heavy leather glove with copper-tipped talons. It is designed to build a massive static charge to scramble Wi-Fi and make the islanders' clothing stick together.
  • The Feline-Sight Visor (Eyes/Head): A bulky, multi-lensed headset featuring metal cat ears. It allows Smyder to see through solid objects to locate hidden snacks and secret tuna stashes.
  • The Sonic-Hiss Chestpiece (Body): A mechanical plate with a brass cat-mouth speaker. It emits a low-frequency vibration that scares away dogs and causes ice cream to melt instantly.

1. The Static-Claw Gauntlet (Hands)

Smyder wears this heavy, leather-and-brass glove over his right hand. It is covered in copper wiring and ends in four sharp, metallic talons. By dragging the claws across the volcanic sand of the beach, Smyder can build up a massive static charge.

  • The Nefarious Purpose: He uses the built-up electricity to cause every television on the island to show only static and to make the islanders' laundry stick together in giant, unwearable clumps.

2. The Feline-Sight Visor (Eyes/Head)

This is a bulky, wraparound headset with multiple glowing lenses. It looks like a combination of high-tech night vision and old-fashioned binoculars, accented with small metal "ears" on the top.

  • The Nefarious Purpose: This visor allows Smyder to see through walls to find exactly where the islanders have hidden their secret stashes of gourmet tuna and fancy yarn. Once located, he sends his cats in to liberate the goods.

3. The Sonic-Hiss Chestpiece (Body)

A medium-sized mechanical plate that Smyder straps over his chest. It features a large, brass speaker shaped like a roaring cat's mouth and several glowing canisters of compressed air on the sides.

  • The Nefarious Purpose: When activated, it emits a low-frequency "mega-hiss" that doesn't hurt ears but causes every dog on the island to hide under the bed and every ice cream cone to melt instantly from the vibration.

Three-Week Timeline

Week 1: Exploration. We will create three distinct 3D block-outs focusing on silhouette and primary colors. By the end of the week, they select one version to develop further.
Week 2: Technical Build. We will refine their chosen block-out into a high-detail mesh. This stage focuses on mechanical logic, including bolts, straps, and seams that make the prop look wearable.
Week 3: Texturing. We will apply materials that tell a story. This includes adding scratches, salt-air rust, and "nefarious" grime to the prop before a final render is produced.

Week 7: The Multi-Draft Phase

  • Focus: 3D Block-outs and Color Theory.
  • Lesson: Silhouette and Primary Forms. Students learn how to identify the "big shapes" of a prop and how those shapes communicate the item's purpose.
  • Benchmark: The Triple Block-out. Students must create three different rough versions of their chosen prop in 3D.
  • Deliverable: A single render or screenshot showing all three variations. Each model should have basic block-out geometry and primary colors applied to show the intended palette. By the end of the week, the student must select one of the three to move forward.

Week 2: The Structural Refinement

Focus: Detailing and Functional Design.
Lesson: Mechanical Logic. How do the pieces fit together? Students will focus on secondary forms like straps, bolts, lenses, and wires.
Benchmark: The High-Detail Mesh. Students refine their chosen block-out into a final 3D model.
Deliverable: A wireframe and shaded render of the refined prop. The model should look "buildable," meaning it has clear joints, seams, and attachment points for a human wearer.
Week 9: The Final Polish" class="w-full h-full object-cover transition-transform duration-700 hover:scale-110">

Week 9: The Final Polish

  • Focus: Surfaces and Storytelling Textures.
  • Lesson: Material Narrative. Students learn how to add "character" to an object through wear and tear. Is the leather scuffed? Is the metal rusted from the island's salt air?
  • Benchmark: Final PBR Texturing and Presentation.
  • Deliverable: A final presentation image featuring the finished prop with full textures and lighting. They must also include a brief technical note explaining which part of the prop's design is specifically tailored to fit a human boy.

Project Marking Rubric

Design and Concept (25 points)
Successful completion of three distinct 3D block-outs in week 1. The designs must show a clear "nefarious" intent and fit the specific proportions of a human boy.
Technical Execution (30 points)
Refinement of the chosen prop with clean 3D geometry. The model should include functional details like straps, buttons, or lenses that explain how the object is worn and operated.
Material Storytelling (25 points)
Application of textures that show age and personality. Marks are awarded for adding realistic wear such as salt-air rust, scratches from cats, or scuffed surfaces.
Final Delivery (20 points)
A high-quality final render with professional lighting, along with the blender file.

1. Ideation and Block-outs (25 Points)

  • Did we produce three distinct 3D variations in Week 1?!
  • Do the initial block-outs demonstrate a clear understanding of the prop’s intended purpose?
  • Are the primary colors and shapes balanced and effective for a "nefarious" theme?

2. Modeling and Structural Logic (30 Points)

  • Does the refined mesh show professional modeling techniques (clean geometry)?

  • Are secondary details like fasteners, wires, or joints present and logically placed?

  • Is the scale appropriate for a medium-sized wearable for a human boy?

3. Material Storytelling (25 Points)

  • Do the final textures show realistic wear and tear (e.g., scuffed leather or weathered metal)?

  • Does the "material narrative" support the story of Adventure Island (e.g., salt-water corrosion)?

  • Is the use of color and finish consistent with the character of Smyder?

4. Final Presentation and Technical Fit (20 Points)

  • Is the final render clear, well-lit, and professionally composed?

  • Does we provide a brief explanation of how the prop is ergonomically designed for a human wearer?

  • Is the final submission polished and free of major technical glitches?
