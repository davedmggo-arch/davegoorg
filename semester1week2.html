Week 2: Grease Pencil Tools & Technical Drawing

This week focuses on mastering Grease Pencil’s drawing tools, organizing layers, and producing precise technical drawings. Students will create multi-view orthographic projections (front, side, top) of a simple object, applying correct line weights, proportions, and materials. The goal is to bridge freehand drawing with technical accuracy, preparing students for 3D modeling.

Key Concepts & Tools


Customizing Shortcuts
Why Shortcuts Matter
  • Faster switching between tools.
  • Builds consistent workflow habits early.
  • Shortcuts persist across lessons, reinforcing muscle memory.

How to Assign Shortcuts
  • Right-click on any tool in the toolbar.
  • Select “Assign Shortcut.”
  • Press the key you want to use.

Example Setup for Grease Pencil
  • Draw Tool → 1
  • Erase Tool → 2
  • Simple, memorable, and serves as the baseline for all lessons.
Navigating the Workspace
Blender is a 3D program, but Grease Pencil work is 2D-focused. To use it effectively, you must learn how to control the view.
  • Orbit View → Alt + Click + Drag
  • Reset View → Click Camera Icon (right side of viewport)
  • Pan View → Alt + Shift + Click + Drag
  • Zoom View → Alt + Ctrl + Click + Drag
    Zooming out allows you to check overall composition. Great art balances detail with the whole canvas.
  • Frame All (Home) → Show all objects.
  • Frame Selected (Numpad .) → Focus on selected object.
  • Front Orthographic View (Numpad 1) → Best for Grease Pencil drawing.
File Organization & Save Disciplines
Why It Matters
  • Blender can crash. Frequent saving prevents big losses.
  • Organized files = smoother workflow and cleaner submissions.
Recommended Folder Setup for ANI106
  • Main folder: ANI106
  • Subfolder: Project 0
  • Store all project files and submission assets inside Project 0.
Saving Best Practices
  • Save often.
  • Use Save Incremental (File → Save As → Save Incremental) to create numbered versions: project0_v01, v02, etc.
  • Never overwrite a good version. Versioning protects your progress.
Objects & Layers Setup
Objects
  • Each prop = its own Grease Pencil Object.
  • Example: Rename an object “Mug” if you are designing a mug.
  • To create a new object, switch from Draw Mode (top left of canvas) to Object Mode.
  • From Object Mode, create a new Grease Pencil Object → Blank.
  • Rename objects in the Outliner (top right). Grease Pencil objects show as orange squiggly lines. Double-click to rename.

Layers (within each object)
  • Found below the canvas, near the timeline.
  • Construction Layer → Rough sketches, guides.
  • Clean Layer → Final polished linework.
  • To add or remove layers, use the plus (+) and minus (-) buttons above the Grease Pencil timeline.
  • To reorder layers, use the up and down arrow buttons next to the layer creation controls.
  • Tip: When erasing, lock all other layers. Blender’s eraser can affect multiple layers at once if they remain unlocked.
Creating & Applying Materials
Where to Find Materials
  • In the Properties tab (right panel).
  • Click the Materials icon → a red circle with a checker pattern.

Adding New Materials
  • Click the plus (+) button at the top right of the Materials tab.
  • Click New to create a new material.

Why Materials Matter
  • Materials define the color and style of strokes and fills.
  • Colors assigned to a material will update across your entire animation if changed.
  • Example: If your stroke color is black and you switch it to dark red, every stroke using that material updates automatically.

Best Practice
  • Assign unique materials for major elements of your design.
  • Keep naming clear: “Construction_Blue” “Clean_Black” “Notes_Red”
Brush Controls
Radius (Size of Brush)
  • Adjust via the Radius Slider at the top toolbar.
  • Or press F + Drag to change interactively.

Strength (Opacity/Intensity)
  • Adjust via the Strength Slider at the top toolbar.
  • Or press Shift + F + Drag interactively.
Drawing Exercises Part 1: Freehand Shapes
Why Freehand?
  • Adds character, rhythm, and life to your lines.
  • Encourages expressive, natural shapes over rigid outlines.

Exercise
  • Draw a simple prop (like a mug) using two layers:
  • Construction Layer → rough shapes.
  • Clean Layer → polished strokes.
  • Apply materials for consistent colors.
  • Switch between zoomed-in detail and zoomed-out composition.
  • Experiment with brush radius and strength for varied line feel.
Drawing Exercises Part 2: Orthographic Views
Deliverable for Project 0
  • Draw three orthographic views of three different props.
  • Required views: Front, Side, Top.

Theme Awareness
  • The theme you choose now will guide your later modeling project.

Example:
  • If your theme is Woodland Kitchen, you will be building a kitchen by the end of the semester.
  • If your theme is Cozy Living Room, you will be creating a living room as your final.
  • Choose props that interest you and that fit your chosen theme.
  • If you are unsure, ask questions early. Your prop decisions now carry forward.

What are Orthographic Views?
  • Technical drawings with no perspective.
  • Show precise proportions and relationships between parts.
  • Modelers rely on them to create accurate 3D forms.

Line Weight Hierarchy
  • Silhouette Lines → Thickest, define the outer boundary.
  • Inner Lines → Thinner than silhouette, describe interior details.
  • Faceted Lines → Thinnest or lightest, indicate subtle form shifts.
  • This hierarchy creates clarity and readability for modelers.

Onion Skinning for Alignment
  • Place front, side, and top views on different frames of your clean layer.
  • Enable Onion Skinning → lets you reference boundaries and landmarks from one view to another.
  • Ensures consistency in height, width, and proportions across views.
Timeline & Keyframes
Automatic Keyframes
  • In Grease Pencil, if the red recording button is active, Blender will automatically create a keyframe whenever you draw on a frame with no drawing data.
  • Each keyframe lasts until the next one is created.

Editing Keyframes
  • You can move keyframes along the timeline to adjust timing.
  • This is useful if you misplace a drawing or want to reorganize.

Timeline Control
  • Found at the bottom right of the screen.
  • By default, Blender provides 250 frames.
  • You can shorten this range (for example, to 40 frames) for clarity, so your front, side, and top views appear close together and easy to manage in a single file.
Onion Skin Visibility Settings
Default Behavior
  • Blender shows one keyframe before and after the active frame.

Adjusting Visibility
  • Go to Object Data Properties tab (just above Materials Properties).
  • In the Onion Skin settings, change:
  • Keyframes Before = 2
  • Keyframes After = 2

Why Adjust?
  • Since you are working with three views on three separate keyframes, this setting ensures you always see all your drawings (front, side, top) when flipping between frames.
  • Creates a clearer reference grid for proportion and alignment.
Rendering Your Work
Why Render
In animation classes you will often render movie files, but here we are exporting still images.
Rendering to images helps document your work and prepare it for submission.

Where to Render
Go to the Output Properties tab (printer icon).
In the Output Settings, change File Format from PNG to JPEG.

Output Directory
By default, Blender saves renders to a temporary folder.
Change the output directory to the Project 0 folder you created earlier.
This keeps your renders organized with the rest of your files.

What to Render
Render one orthographic view from one prop per JPEG.
Since you have three props and three orthographic views per prop, you will end up with nine images for this project.

Homework

Final Technical Drawing Set: Complete front, side, and top views of your chosen objects; check for proportional consistency.
Final Submission Package:
  • Organized Blender file(s).
  • PNG/JPG renders of drawings and animation.
  • Workflow documentation.
  • Submit by end of Week 3.
