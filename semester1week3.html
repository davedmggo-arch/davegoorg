Week 3: Drawing Three Orthographic Views of a Mug

In this class, you will learn how to create accurate and visually compelling orthographic views of props, with a focus on designing a mug that carries forward into your thematic environment project. The session will introduce the ideation process of developing a mug design that reflects larger environment themes, establish construction techniques for consistent proportions, and emphasize shape language as a key design principle. Students will practice layering, line hierarchy, and color coding to separate construction, clean lines, and notes. By the end of class, you will understand how to use orthographic drawings to clearly communicate design intent for future modeling work while developing a strong workflow.

  • Using guides and centerlines to block in the mug’s front, side, and top views.

Ideation and Theme Awareness
Before drawing, decide what type of mug you want to design. The mug needs to feel interesting enough to carry your chosen theme forward into future projects.

Four Basic Themes for Mugs
  • Functional/Practical Mug: A standard design focused on usability. Example: a sturdy coffee mug.
  • Stylized/Playful Mug: A mug with exaggerated shapes or whimsical proportions. Example: a mushroom-shaped Woodland Kitchen mug.
  • Elegant/Decorative Mug: A refined design with flowing curves or ornate handles. Example: a porcelain tea cup for a Living Room theme.
  • Experimental/Hybrid Mug: A creative take that blends unusual forms. Example: a mug shaped like a log, integrated with the Woodland Kitchen environment.

In this example, my mug will be used by a woodworking lumberjack who has a love of axes and trees!
Construction Lines and Proportion Setup
Orthographic drawings are technical, so proportion accuracy is critical.

Why Construction Lines Matter
  • Establishes the framework of the mug.
  • Maintains consistent proportions across the front, side, and top views.
  • Helps later when adding details and refining design.

Process
  • Start with large geometric primitives (cylinders, rectangles, ellipses).
  • Draw centerlines vertically and horizontally to anchor symmetry.
  • Add guides for handle placement and mug thickness.
  • Reuse these guides in all three views for consistent alignment.

Color Coding for Clarity
  • Blue = construction lines.
  • Black = clean final lines.
  • Red = notes, callouts, or corrections.
Shape Hierarchy: Large vs. Small Details
Understanding detail frequency will keep the design clear and readable.

Large Details
  • Overall mug silhouette.
  • Basic handle shape and rim thickness.

Medium Details
  • Decorative ridges, slight contour variations.
  • Handle curve thickness, base support.

Small Details
  • Fine textures, subtle surface accents, small engravings.

Rule: Start large → refine to medium → finish with small.
Frequency Principle: Too many small details can clutter the design; balance is key.
Design Principles and Shape Language
Shape language communicates the personality of your mug and the theme of your environment.

Circles and Curves
  • Friendly, soft, approachable.
  • Works well in cozy, playful, or organic designs.

Squares and Rectangles
  • Stable, sturdy, reliable.
  • Good for functional mugs with industrial or modern themes.

Triangles and Angled Shapes
  • Dynamic, sharp, aggressive.
  • Use sparingly in mugs, but effective for experimental or stylized designs.

Application: If designing for a Woodland Kitchen, use rounded organic curves. For a Living Room theme, use smooth rectangles with stable bases.
Line Weight Hierarchy
Different line types explain different aspects of the form.

Silhouette Lines
  • Thickest lines.
  • Define the outermost boundary of the mug.

Inner Lines
  • Medium thickness.
  • Show important structural features inside the silhouette (like handle attachment points or the rim edge).

Faceted Lines
  • Thinnest or lightest lines.
  • Indicate subtle surface changes or form shifts (like small bevels or slight indents).
  • Should not overpower silhouette or inner lines.

Tip: This hierarchy improves clarity for modelers and makes your drawings read from a distance.
Consistency Across Views
The final step is ensuring all three views line up and communicate the same object.

Use Onion Skinning
  • Check the front, side, and top views against each other by toggling onion skinning.

Check Proportions
  • Height and width of the mug body should remain identical across views.
  • Handle placement must align in both front and side views.

Repeat Construction Lines
  • Transfer centerlines, mug width, and rim thickness consistently across all frames.

Final Deliverable:
  • Three orthographic views (front, side, top) of your mug.
  • Blue construction lines, black clean lines, red notes if necessary.
  • Clear silhouette, inner, and faceted lines with proper hierarchy.
