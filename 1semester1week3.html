Week 3: Lighting, Composition & Rendering

You will act as a cinematographer, implementing professional 3-point lighting and rule-of-thirds composition to render a high-quality portfolio piece.

Start Learning

Learning Outcomes

  • Implement a professional 3-point lighting setup to create depth and dimension in a 3D scene.
  • Apply the Rule of Thirds and focal length adjustments to create dynamic, cinematic compositions.
  • Configure render settings and denoising to produce high-quality final images efficiently.

Key Concepts

1. UV Optimization: The Final Fit

Before moving to lighting, we must ensure our textures will display perfectly without warping or losing quality.

  • Minimizing Distortion: Use the "Display Stretch" overlay in the UV Editor. Blue faces indicate a perfect fit, while yellow or red areas mean the texture will look stretched.
  • Packing the UV Space: Aim to fill the 0-to-1 square as efficiently as possible. Think of this like packing a suitcase; the less empty space you leave, the higher the resolution of your objects.
  • Texel Density: Ensure that your objects have a similar amount of texture space relative to their size. A small screw should not have more texture detail than the entire tabletop.

2. Camera & Composition

In this phase, you act as the cinematographer, choosing how the audience views your work.

  • Focal Length: Adjusting the lens changes the "feel" of the scene. A 50mm lens feels natural, while an 85mm lens (telephoto) compresses space and makes your props look more "heroic."
  • Rule of Thirds: Use the camera grid to place points of interest—like the lamp or a book—along the "power lines" rather than just centering everything.
  • Depth of Field: Use camera settings to slightly blur the background or foreground, which draws the viewer’s eye directly to your focal point.

3. The 3-Point Lighting Setup

Lighting is the key to making 3D models look like cinematic objects rather than flat shapes.

  • Key Light: Your primary light source. It defines the main shapes and creates the strongest shadows.
  • Fill Light: A softer light placed opposite the Key Light. It lifts the dark shadows so detail isn't lost in the "blacks."
  • Rim Light: Placed behind the object, pointing toward the camera. This creates a "halo" or highlight along the edges, separating the model from the background.

4. Render Settings: Final Output

Rendering is the process of calculating light rays to create a final image file.

  • Cycles vs. Eevee: Use Cycles for physically accurate, ray-traced results (high quality), or Eevee for fast, real-time results.
  • Resolution: Set your output to 1920 x 1080 (Full HD) for a professional standard.
  • Denoising: Enable Denoising to remove "salt and pepper" grain from your image. This allows you to get a clean render without waiting hours for high sample counts.

5. Shortcuts & Best Practices

  • Add Light: Shift + A > Light (Area lights are recommended for soft, realistic shadows).
  • Look Through Camera: Numpad 0.
  • Lock Camera to View: Press N (Side Panel) > View > Check "Camera to View" to move the camera by navigating the viewport.
  • Render Image: F12.
  • Color Temperature: Try a warm (orange/yellow) Key light and a cool (blue) Fill light to create professional color contrast.

6. Week 3 Assignment

  • The Task: Create a cinematic render of your table and props.
  • Step 1: Finalize your UV layouts and ensure no textures are distorted.
  • Step 2: Set up a camera using the Rule of Thirds and an appropriate focal length.
  • Step 3: Implement a 3-point lighting system with varying light colors for mood.
  • Submit: A high-quality .PNG or .JPG render of your final scene.
