Week 5: Hard Surface Clothing Creation and Accessories

Following the block out of your character’s head and torso, we now branch into the development of the full character bust. This phase focuses on constructive modeling for limbs, stylistic hair, and the technical workflows used by professional studios to prepare models for animation.

Essential Technical Foundations

Before you begin adding new components, you must ensure your object data is clean and your origin points are placed with precision. Proper setup prevents your sculpt brushes from feeling oval or distorted.


1. Applying Transforms

  • How to Apply: In Object Mode, select your objects and press Ctrl + A then select Rotation and Scale.
  • Why it matters: This resets the internal math of the object so that a 10cm brush stroke is actually 10cm on every axis.
Pro-Tip: If your Symmetry (X-Mirror) feels "off center" even though the model looks centered, it is almost always because the Rotation or Scale hasn't been applied.

2. Origin Points and Control

The Origin Point (the small orange dot) is the pivot for all movement, rotation, and scaling. For character components, placing this accurately is vital for modifiers like Mirror to work correctly.

  • Setting the Origin: Right-click an object and navigate to Set Origin. You can choose "Origin to Geometry" to center it or "Origin to 3D Cursor" for custom placement.
  • Precision Control: Use Shift + S to snap the 3D Cursor to a specific vertex or the world center (0,0,0) before setting your origin to that cursor.
  • Pivot Point: Your origin determines how an object rotates; for example, an eyeball's origin must be at its exact geometric center to rotate naturally in the socket.
Pro-Tip: Always keep your character's main body origin at the world center (0,0,0). This acts as the "anchor" for every other accessory you create.

1. Constructive Appendages: Arms and Hands

  • Arm Blocking: Use cylinders or elongated spheres to define upper arm and forearm on one side of your character. Position these in a standard A-pose to ensure shoulders can deform naturally during animation.
  • Mitten Method: Start with a squashed cube for palm. Simplifying hand into a solid mitten shape is an excellent way to maintain a strong graphic silhouette while keeping topology manageable.
  • Fusing Limbs: Once primitives are positioned and you are happy with the silhouette, press Ctrl + J to Join them and Ctrl + R to Voxel Remesh connection points.
  • Mirror Modifier: After fusing arm to torso, add Mirror Modifier to character. Use Eyedropper tool to select Body/Torso as Mirror Object. This ensures fused arm mirrors perfectly across world center.
Pro-Tip: When modeling A-pose, keep a slight bend in elbow. This "pre-bend" helps rigger later by showing computer exactly which way arm is supposed to fold.

2: Standard Character Accessories: Hair, Eyes, and Lashes

  • Eyeballs: Add UV Sphere but do not fuse it to head mesh. Keeping eyes as separate objects allows them to rotate during animation.
  • Face Project for Brows and Lashes: Start with simple Plane. Enable snapping, set snapping type to Face Project, and ensure Project Individual Elements is checked. This allows you to move vertices directly onto surface of your sculpt.
  • Extrusion Safety Rule: You must turn Face Project snapping OFF before you extrude (E) brows or lashes outward. If you leave it on, snapping logic will fight you and lock new faces back into mesh.
  • Subdivided Cube Hair: Instead of using sculpt brushes to pull out strands, start with Subdivided Cube (Create a cube and press Ctrl + 2 to create a Subdivision Modifier with 2 Levels). Use Loop Cuts (Ctrl + R in Edit Mode) to add segments and move vertices to form individual clumps or strands. This provides superior control over topology and maintains clean quads.
  • Quad Rule: Always model using four sided faces (Quads). Avoid triangles or N-gons. Keeping faces as quads is an industry standard habit that ensures models can be subdivided and animated without visual glitches.
Pro-Tip: For graphic hair, follow "Large, Medium, Small" rule. Create one or two large primary masses, three or four medium clumps, and a few small "stray" strands to create visual interest and rhythm.

3: Method 1 (The Refined Primitive Block-out)

This method is perfect for tubular items such as sleeves, gauntlets, or leggings. It is fast and ensures your clothing follows the natural volume of the limbs.


Step 1: Starting Lean with Primitives

Press (Shift + A) and select a Cylinder or UV Sphere. In the Add menu at the bottom left, set your vertices to 8 or 12. Working with low polygon counts makes the initial shaping much easier to manage. If the primitive has extra horizontal rings you do not need, select the edge loop with (Alt + Left Click), press X, and choose Dissolve Edges.


Step 2: Aligning to the Model Sheet

In Edit Mode, rotate and scale your primitive until it matches the angle of the limb on your front model sheet. Use Loop Cuts (Ctrl + R) to add geometry only where the silhouette changes, such as the bulge of a muscle or a taper at the wrist. After placing a cut, hit S to scale it immediately to fit the sheet.


Step 3: Designing for Proper Deformation

It is vital that your loops are clean circles around the limbs. Avoid spiral topology, as this will cause the mesh to collapse during animation. Ensure you have at least three loop cuts at bending points like elbows or knees. This allows the mesh to accordion properly when the character moves.


Step 4: Refining with Bevels

To give your primitive a manufactured look, select the top and bottom rims of the gear and press (Ctrl + B). This adds a Bevel, creating a small angled face that catches the light and makes the object look like a solid piece of hard surface equipment.

4: Method 2 (The Expanded Plane-to-Volume Approach)

This method is fantastic for high-precision pieces like breastplates, shoulder pads, or tactical vests. We will build the clothing from the front, wrap it to the back, and then bridge the gap.


Step 1: Tracing the Front Silhouette

In Object Mode, press (Shift + A) and add a Plane. Rotate it 90 degrees on the X-axis so it faces you. In Edit Mode, move your vertices to match the front-view model sheet. Use Extrude (E) to build out the flat panel of the garment. Remember: Always work with Quads (four-sided faces) to ensure the mesh deforms smoothly later!


Step 2: Creating the Backside

Once your front panel is complete, follow these steps to create the back:

  • Duplicate: Select all geometry with the A key and press (Shift + D).
  • Position: Move the duplicate along the Y-axis until it sits behind your character model, aligned with your back model sheet.
  • Flip Normals: Because we duplicated the front, the back faces are pointing the wrong way. Press (Alt + N) and select Flip so the outside of the mesh faces away from the body.

Step 3: Bridging the Seams

Now we need to connect the two pieces to create a solid object. Select the edge loops on the sides of the front and back panels where a side seam would naturally occur. Right-click and choose Bridge Edge Loops. This creates the side panels of your clothing instantly.


Step 4: Shaping and Sculpting

Switch to the Side View (Numpad 3). Use Loop Cuts (Ctrl + R) to add horizontal detail. This allows you to pull the middle of the mesh forward or backward to match the depth of your character. Finally, switch to Sculpt Mode and use the Grab Brush to subtly nudge the armor so it fits snugly against the base model without clipping.

5: The Final Modifier Stack

Once your base mesh is complete using either method, we apply the final modifiers to turn our low poly work into a professional finished product. The order of these modifiers is very important.


  1. Mirror: This stays at the top of the stack to maintain symmetry.
  2. Solidify: Add this next to give your clothing physical thickness. Adjust the Thickness value to about 0.02. Set the Offset to 1.0 to grow the thickness outward, ensuring it does not clip into the character skin.
  3. Subdivision Surface: Press (Ctrl + 2) to add this at the bottom. This smooths out the edges and makes the model look high quality.

Pro Tip: Support Loops

If the Subdivision Surface modifier makes your armor look too round or mushy, go back into Edit Mode. Add Loop Cuts very close to your sharp edges. These act as support loops that tell the modifier to keep that specific area crisp and hard.
