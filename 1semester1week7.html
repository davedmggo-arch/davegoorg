Week 7: Interior Design: Blocking

You will establish the architectural scale of a room through "blocking," focusing on creating a layout that effectively guides the camera and actor.

Start Learning

About This Course

  • Construct a 3D architectural "shell" using real-world dimensions to ensure environmental scale accuracy.
  • Design an "acting space" by strategically placing furniture to support camera movement and character paths.
  • Apply composition fundamentals and camera focal lengths to establish the visual story of an interior room.

Key Concepts

1. Room Geometry: The Foundation

Blocking, also known as "grey-boxing," is the process of using simple shapes to establish the scale and flow of a room before adding any high-level detail.

  • Modeling the Shell: Start with a simple plane for the floor and extrude the edges upward to create the walls and ceiling.
  • Real-World Scale: It is vital to model at real-world dimensions. In Blender, ensure your walls are an appropriate height—usually between 2.4m and 3m—so that lights and cameras behave realistically.
  • Architectural Features: Use the Knife tool or Boolean operations to cut out holes for essential features like windows and doorways.

2. Acting Space: Planning for Performance

An interior environment in animation is more than just a room; it is a stage. It must be designed to accommodate the movements of your characters and the camera.

  • Furniture Placement: Use simple cubes and cylinders to represent furniture. Position them to create "paths" for a character to walk through.
  • The Fourth Wall: Avoid building a perfect four-walled box. Often, removing one or two walls makes it much easier to position your camera and lighting equipment.
  • Sightlines: Arrange props to guide the viewer's eye and ensure the "stage" remains uncluttered so the camera can move freely.

3. Composition & Framing

This week is about architecture and layout, but you must plan your camera angles early to ensure the room looks good from the perspectives that matter.

  • Camera Angles: Set up your primary camera early. Consider how different heights and angles change the "feel" of the interior.
  • Focal Points: Use your blocked furniture and architectural lines (like a hallway or the edge of a rug) to lead the eye toward the center of the action.
  • Scale Reference: Periodically place a simple "human-sized" cube (approx. 1.8m tall) in your room to ensure your furniture isn't accidentally giant or tiny.

4. Essential Shortcuts & Tools

  • Walk Navigation: Press Shift + ~ (Tilde) to "walk" through your room in first-person view to check the scale and paths.
  • Create Face: Select two edges and press F to bridge them, creating a wall or floor section.
  • Join Objects: Select multiple separate pieces and press Ctrl + J to combine them into one mesh for easier management.
  • Snap to Grid: Hold Ctrl while moving (G) to snap objects to the floor grid for precise placement.
  • X-Ray Mode: Press Alt + Z to select vertices on both the front and back of your walls simultaneously.

5. Best Practices for Blocking

  • Keep it Simple: Do not add textures or small details like doorknobs yet. Focus entirely on the large shapes and the volume of the room.
  • Naming Conventions: Immediately rename your objects in the Outliner (e.g., "Wall_North," "Table_Blockout") to stay organized as the scene grows.
  • Check Your Normals: Ensure the "inside" of your walls is facing the center of the room so that lighting renders correctly in later weeks.

6. Week 7 Assignment

  • The Task: Submit the blocked room layout.
  • Step 1: Model the walls, floor, and ceiling of a single room to scale.
  • Step 2: Place grey-box furniture to define the acting space and camera paths.
  • Step 3: Set up at least one camera angle that follows the rule of thirds.
  • Submit: A screenshot of your blocked room layout from the 3D Viewport (mesh only).
