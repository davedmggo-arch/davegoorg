Week 6: Advanced Surface Painting

In this lesson, we will focus on the visual presentation of your character. We will cover material assignment, vertex color data, procedural blending, lighting setups.

1. Material Slots and Multi-Material Assignment

To have different colors or textures on a single mesh (such as a leather vest with metal buckles), you must use Material Slots.

  • Material Preview Mode: Change your 3D viewport's shader mode to Material Preview Mode. This will ensure that you are able to see your materials as you create and manipulate them.

  • The Material Tab: With your clothing selected, go to the Material Properties (red sphere icon).

  • Creating Slots: Click the plus (+) icon on the right side of the material list to create a new slot. Click New to assign a material to that slot.

  • Assigning to Geometry: Switch to Edit Mode. Select the specific faces you want to change (e.g., the shoulder plates). Select the desired material in your list and click the Assign button.

View Gallery (3)

1. Material Slots: Organizing Your Design

Most complex clothing consists of different materials. For example, a tactical vest might have a Cordura fabric body, plastic buckles, and metal zippers. You do not need separate objects for these; you use Material Slots.

  • The Slot List: Located in the Material Properties tab (the red sphere icon), this list acts as a container.

  • Adding Multiple Materials: Click the + button to create a new slot, then click + New to create a material for that slot. Name them clearly (e.g., "Vest_Fabric," "Buckle_Metal").

  • The Assignment Workflow: 1. Enter Edit Mode (Tab). 2. Select the faces you want to change. 3. Select the specific material in the list. 4. Click the Assign button located just below the list.

2. The Core Shader Settings (Principled BSDF)

Once a material is assigned, you will primarily work with the Principled BSDF node. This is a "Physically Based Rendering" (PBR) shader designed to mimic how light interacts with real-world surfaces.


Base Color

This is the fundamental color of your surface.

  • Pro Tip: For realism, avoid using 100% white or 100% black. In the real world, "black" charcoal still reflects a bit of light, and "white" snow is never perfectly pure. Stay within the 0.05 to 0.8 value range for the best results.

Metallic

This is a binary setting: a surface is either a metal or it isn't.

  • Value 0.0: For "Dielectric" materials like fabric, plastic, wood, or skin.

  • Value 1.0: For "Conductor" materials like steel, gold, or chrome.

  • Note: Avoid "in-between" values like 0.5 unless you are modeling something very specific, like a metallic paint or a very dirty, rusted metal.

Roughness

This determines how "sharp" the reflections are.

  • 0.0 (Smooth): Creates a mirror-like finish. Excellent for polished chrome or wet surfaces.

  • 1.0 (Rough): Creates a matte, diffuse finish. Ideal for dry fabrics, rubber, or rough stone.

  • Hard-Surface Tip: Most hard-surface gear looks best around 0.3 to 0.5 to catch nice highlights without looking like a mirror.

View Image

3. Subsurface (The Basic Setup)

While we are focusing on hard-surface gear, you will likely have a character's skin visible underneath. This is where Subsurface Scattering (SSS) is essential.

  • Subsurface Weight: This determines how much light "soaks" into the surface. For human skin, a value between 0.05 and 0.1 is usually enough.

  • Subsurface Radius: This tells Blender how far different colors of light travel under the surface. Since blood is red, the red channel (the first of the three values) should usually be the highest.

  • Subsurface Color: Set this to a warm, fleshy tone. This gives the skin that "living" glow, preventing it from looking like cold, hard plastic.

2. Color Attributes and Vertex Painting

Color Attributes (formerly Vertex Colors) allow you to store color data directly on the vertices of your mesh without needing a complex UV map or an external texture file.

  • Switch to Sculpt Mode or Vertex Paint Mode: At the top left of the viewport, change the mode to Vertex Paint.

  • Painting the Mesh: Use the brush to paint colors directly onto the geometry.

  • The Shader Link: To see these colors in your final render, you must go to the Shader Editor, add a Color Attribute node, and connect the "Color" output to the "Base Color" of your Principled BSDF.

View Gallery (2)

1. Creating Multiple Attribute Layers

Before painting, you need to set up unique containers for different types of data.

  • The Data Tab: With your model selected, go to the Data Properties (green triangle icon).

  • Color Attributes List: Find the Color Attributes section. Click the plus (+) icon to add a new layer.

  • Naming for Logic: Name your layers based on their purpose (e.g., "Mask_Metal" or "Mask_Roughness"). Ensure the Domain is set to Point and the Data Type is set to Color.

View Image

2. Setting Up the Shader Editor for Color Attributes

Before you can see your brush strokes in a render, you must tell Blender's shader how to "read" the data stored on the vertices.

  1. Open the Shader Editor: Select your object and ensure a material is assigned.

  2. Add the Attribute Node: Press Shift + A and search for Color Attribute.

  3. Select the Map: In the node's drop-down menu, select the attribute layer you created (usually named "Color" by default).

  4. Connect to Base Color: Plug the Color output of the Attribute node into the Base Color input of your Principled BSDF.

View Gallery (3)

3. Vertex Painting and Mesh Density

The most important concept to grasp is that Vertex Painting is limited by your geometry. Unlike a texture map which has pixels, Vertex Paint lives on the vertices themselves. To get a high-quality paint job, you must have enough vertices to support the detail.


Applying Your Modifiers

If your base model is too low-poly, your paint will look blurry and pixelated. To fix this, you should apply your modifiers.

  • The Process: In Object Mode, go to your modifier stack and click the dropdown arrow on your modifiers, then select Apply. Do this for all modifiers from the top, downward.

  • The Benefit: This converts the "procedural" smoothness into actual physical vertices that you can now paint on directly.


The "Lag" Caveat

While more vertices mean more detail, there is a limit to what your hardware can handle.

  • The Limit: I recommend keeping your total scene count below 1 million faces.

  • The Risk: Exceeding this limit can cause significant viewport lag, making it difficult to rotate your camera or use your brushes smoothly. Always try to find the balance between "enough detail to look good" and "low enough to keep the file responsive."

5. Professional Material Assignment Workflow

When using Color Attributes, you often want to combine them with your Material Slots for maximum control.

  1. Assign Base Materials: Use slots to define "Metal" and "Fabric" areas as we discussed in the previous lesson.

  2. Add the Color Attribute: Apply the same Color Attribute node to both the Metal and Fabric shaders.

  3. The "Dirt" Layer: Now, when you paint a dark brown color across the entire model in Vertex Paint Mode, it will look like mud is caked onto both the metal buckles and the fabric straps simultaneously.

6. Painting Techniques: Additional Tools

To get the most out of your painting, use these shortcuts in Vertex Paint Mode:

  • Blur Brush: Use this to soften the transitions between colors if your mesh density is causing "stepping" or jagged edges.

  • Secondary Colour: Hold (Ctrl) to use your brush's secondary colour.

  • The X Toggle: Use the X Symmetry at the top right (just like in Sculpt Mode) to ensure your paint job stays perfectly symmetrical across your character's clothing.

3. The Shader Editor: Mix Color and Procedural Masking

To create professional surfaces, we often need to blend different colors or textures together. We do this using the Mix Color node.

  • Blending Colors: In the Shader Editor (Shift + A > Color > Mix Color), plug two different colors or textures into inputs A and B.

  • Blending Modes: Just like in Photoshop, you can change the dropdown from "Mix" to Multiply, Overlay, or Add.

    • Multiply is excellent for adding dirt or shadows.

    • Overlay is great for adding subtle color variations.

  • Procedural Masking: You can use your Color Attribute as the Factor (Fac) input. This tells Blender to use "Color A" where you painted white and "Color B" where you painted black, creating a procedural mask that defines where wear and tear appear.

View Image

1. Accessing and Navigating the Shader Editor

To begin, you need to change your workspace to the Shading tab at the top of the Blender window. Alternatively, you can turn any window into a Shader Editor by clicking the icon in the top-left corner of any panel and selecting the Shader Editor icon (a small node-and-wire symbol).

Organizing Your Node Tree

As you add more detail, your node tree can become cluttered. Follow these professional organization tips:

  • G to Move: Select a node and press G to grab and move it.

  • Frames (f): Select a group of related nodes (like all your metal settings) and press f to put them in a labeled box.

  • Reroute Points: If your wires are crossing and becoming messy, hold Shift and right-click-drag across a wire to create a "Reroute" dot. This allows you to bend the wire around other nodes.

  • Keep it Linear: Generally, try to keep your data flowing from left (inputs like attributes and textures) to right (the final Material Output).

2. The Mix Color Node: The Foundation of Blending

The Mix Color node (Shift + A > Color > Mix Color) is the primary way to combine two visual inputs. By default, it uses the Mix blending mode.

Separating Two Colors

When the mode is set to Mix, the node simply places Color B on top of Color A.

  • Input A: This is your base color (for example, a clean forest green for a shirt).

  • Input B: This is your secondary color (for example, a dark brown for mud).

  • Factor (Fac): This slider determines the transparency. At 0.0, you see only the green. At 1.0, you see only the mud.

  • The Power of Masks: Instead of using the slider, plug your Color Attribute node into the Fac input. Now, wherever you painted white on your model, the mud will appear, and wherever you painted black, the original green remains.

Crucial Caveat: The Black and White Rule When using Color Attributes as a mask, you must paint strictly in Grayscale (Black and White). Because the Factor input only understands values between 0.0 (Black) and 1.0 (White), painting in actual colors like red or blue will result in an "in-between" value based on that color's brightness. This can lead to unpredictable blending and may confuse your workflow. Think of your brush as a "transparency brush" rather than a color brush!

3. Beyond Simple Mixing: Blending Modes

Just like in a 2D painting application, you can change how these colors interact by selecting a different Blending Mode from the dropdown menu in the Mix Color node.

Common Blending Modes and Use Cases

Multiply This mode takes the brightness of Color B and multiplies it by Color A. Since 0 is black, anything multiplied by black becomes black.

  • Best Use Case: Adding deep grime, shadows, or "AO" (Ambient Occlusion) into the crevices of your hard-surface armor to make it look weathered.

Add This mode adds the color values together. Adding colors usually results in a brighter final result.

  • Best Use Case: Layering glows or highlights. If you want a specific part of a metal plate to look like it is catching an intense laser light, Add will brighten the base color significantly.

Overlay This mode combines Multiply and Screen. it makes light areas lighter and dark areas darker while preserving the midtones.

  • Best Use Case: Adding subtle "surface noise" or color variation. If your shirt looks too perfectly flat, an Overlay of a grainy texture will give it a natural, non-digital feeling.

Linear Light This is a very high-contrast mode that shifts the colors based on the brightness of the second input.

  • Best Use Case: Creating "Heat Tint" on metal. If you want a gun barrel or an exhaust pipe to look like it has been burnt by high temperatures, using Linear Light with a purple or blue gradient can create that intense, iridescent look.

4. Chaining Mix Nodes for Complexity

You are not limited to just two colors. Professional artists "chain" these nodes together. For example, you might have one Mix Color node set to Mix to add a basic camo pattern, and then plug the output of that node into another Mix Color node set to Multiply to add dirt on top of the camo.

By building your material in layers, you can create gear that looks like it has a history of use in the field.

4. Making it Pop: The Compositor and Glare

In Blender 5.0, the "glow" effect (bloom) is often handled during the render process or in the Compositor for maximum control.

  • Enable Use Nodes: Switch to the Compositor workspace and check the Use Nodes box at the top.

  • The Glare Node: Press Shift + A and search for the Glare node. Place it between your Render Layers node and your Composite node.

  • Setting the Style: Change the Glare type from "Streaks" to Fog Glow.

  • Strength: This setting determines how bright a your emissive material is.

  • Real-time Compositing: In Blender, you can see this effect directly in the 3D Viewport by setting the Viewport Shading menu to Compositor: Always.

View Gallery (3)

1. Overview of the Compositor

The Compositor is a node-based editor used for image processing. Instead of calculating how light bounces off a surface, it takes the pixels already rendered by your camera and applies filters to them.

Think of it as a built-in version of Photoshop that updates every time you hit the render button. We will dive much deeper into the advanced features of the Compositor in our next project, but for now, we will focus on making your emissive materials truly shine.

What can the Compositor do?

  • Color Grading: You can shift the mood of your render to look warm and cinematic or cold and clinical.

  • Lens Effects: You can add "Lens Distortion" to mimic a real-world camera or "Vignetting" to draw the eye to the center of the frame.

  • Depth of Field: You can blur the background more intensely than the camera settings allow.

  • Glow and Glare: You can turn bright pixels into beautiful light bleeds.

View Gallery (3)

2. Real-Time Node Snapping

As your compositor tree grows, keeping your "pipes" (the wires connecting nodes) organized is essential. Blender features a helpful Node Snapping system to keep your workspace clean.

  • The Feature: When you drag a node near a wire, Blender can automatically "snap" the node onto that wire, connecting the input and output sockets instantly.

  • How to Use: Hold the Alt key while dragging a node over an existing wire. The wire will highlight, and when you release the mouse, the node will be perfectly inserted into the chain.

  • Auto-Offset: Ensure Node Auto-offset is enabled in the header of the editor. This moves existing nodes out of the way when you drop a new one in between them, preventing overlapping "spaghetti" nodes.

3. Creating the Bloom Effect

To make your glowing gear look professional, we need to add a glow that bleeds into the surrounding air. In Blender 5.0, we achieve this using the Glare node set to Bloom.

Setting Up the Node

  1. Enable Use Nodes: At the top of the Compositor window, check the Use Nodes box.

  2. Add Glare: Press Shift + A and search for Glare.

  3. The Connection: Place the Glare node between your Render Layers node and your Composite node.

  4. Switch to Bloom: Change the dropdown menu inside the Glare node from "Streaks" to Bloom.

Tuning the Glow

  • Strength: This is the most important setting. Think of this node as a multiplier, where 1 is the default value of emissive materials. Increasing or decreasing this value will increase

  • Size: This controls how far the glow spreads. For a subtle, high-tech look, keep this value lower. For a magical or radioactive effect, increase it.

4. Visualizing the Result in the Viewport

One of the best features of the modern compositor is the ability to see your post-processing effects without waiting for a final render.

5. Professional 3-Point Lighting

To showcase your hard-surface work, you need a setup that highlights the silhouette and surface details.


  1. Key Light: Place this 45 degrees to the side and slightly above the character. This is your primary light source that defines the form.

  2. Fill Light: Place this on the opposite side of the Key Light with lower intensity. This "fills" the deep shadows, ensuring we don't lose detail in the dark areas.

  3. Rim Light (Backlight): Place this behind the character, pointing toward the back of the head and shoulders. This creates a bright "rim" of light that separates the character from the background, which is essential for making hard-surface edges "pop."

View Gallery (3)

1. The Power of Area Lights

Area lights are 2D planes that emit light from a specific surface area. Unlike Point lights, which are infinitely small, Area lights allow you to control the "softness" of your shadows.

  • Size Matters: A small Area light (e.g., 0.5m) will create very sharp, harsh shadows, which is great for high-contrast, aggressive looks. A large Area light (e.g., 2.0m) will create soft, wrapping shadows that are more flattering for skin and fabric.

  • Size and Power Relationship: The right size and power of your lights depend on the scale of your model. If your object is large, then you'll need much more power to light it. For something as big as 2.0m, a light with a size of 1 and a power of 40 is more than enough to properly light it.

View Gallery (3)

2. Intentional Color Selection

Avoid using pure white light. In the real world, every light source has a temperature or a hue. Adding color to your lights creates a "color story" for your character.

  • Complementary Schemes: A classic professional look is the "Orange and Teal" setup. Use a warm orange for your Key Light and a cool blue for your Fill or Rim lights.

  • Environmental Moods: * Cyberpunk: Use high-saturation Magentas and Cyans.

    • Natural Sunlight: Use a pale yellow Key light with a soft blue Fill light to mimic the sky.

    • Horror/Suspense: Use a sickly desaturated green or a deep, moody red.

3. Positioning the 3-Point Setup

This is your strongest light. Place it approximately 45 degrees to the side of the character and 45 degrees above the eye line.

  • Purpose: It defines the primary shapes and creates the "core shadow" on the opposite side of the face or armor.

  • Color: Usually the most "natural" color of the scene (e.g., warm white or pale yellow).

The Fill Light (The Detail Recovery)

Place this on the opposite side of the Key Light, usually at a lower height.

  • Purpose: Its job is to "fill" the dark shadows created by the Key Light. It should be significantly lower in intensity (Power) than the Key Light.

  • Color: Use a cooler or contrasting color here to add depth to the shadows.

The Rim Light (The Silhouette Creator)

Place this behind the character, angled toward their back or side.

  • Purpose: This creates a bright "halo" or rim of light along the edges of the character. This is vital for hard-surface modeling because it makes the metallic edges and silhouettes "pop" against a dark background.

  • Color: This is a great place to use a bold, saturated color that matches your character's "energy" or emissive gear.

View Gallery (2)

4. Defining Character through Light

The way you place these lights should tell us who your character is.

  • The Hero: Use high-placed lights and soft shadows to make them look noble and clear.

  • The Villain: Use "Under-lighting" (placing a light below the character pointing up) to create unnatural, menacing shadows.

  • The Tech-Infiltrator: Use very small, high-intensity Area lights with sharp falloffs to mimic a dark, industrial environment where only small lamps or computer screens provide light.

To see your lighting accurately while you work, ensure your Viewport Shading is set to Rendered. If you are using Cycles, you will see the light bounce off the character's armor and onto the ground, providing a much more realistic preview of how your colors are blending.

1. Professional File and Scene Organization

Before you hit that render button, you must ensure your project is clean. In a studio environment, other artists may need to open your file, so clarity is mandatory.

The Organization Checklist

Before submitting, go through your Outliner and verify the following:

  • Character Organization: All parts of your clothing (vest, sleeves, armor) should be parented to the character's base mesh or organized into a "Character_Mesh" collection.

  • Lights Organization: Place all your Area lights into a dedicated "Lighting" collection.

  • Camera Organization: Keep your cameras in a "Cameras" collection.

  • Proper Material Names: Ensure your material slots do not say "Material.001." Rename them to descriptive titles like "M_Tactical_Vest" or "M_Glow_Trim."

2. Setting Up Your Three Render Angles

To fully showcase your hard-surface clothing and 3-point lighting, you are required to submit three specific views. You should use the same lighting setup for all three to maintain visual consistency.

The Required Angles

  1. Front View: This shows the primary silhouette and the main details of the chest and torso.

  2. 3/4 (Three-Quarter) View: This is the most important angle for 3D art. It reveals the depth of your clothing, the thickness from your Solidify modifier, and how the light catches the side edges.

  3. Back View: This shows the rear construction of the garment and how you bridged the seams from Method 1.

Camera Tip

Instead of moving one camera around, I recommend creating three separate cameras. This allows you to jump between views quickly without losing your perfect framing. Press Numpad 0 to toggle the active camera view.
