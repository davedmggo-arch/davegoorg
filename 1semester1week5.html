Week 5: Artistic Character Sculpting — Head & Torso

You will expand your sculpt to the torso, focusing on planar changes and silhouetting .

Start Learning!!

Learning Outcomes

Welcome to Week 5! This week, we are moving beyond basic head shapes to explore the Human Bust. We are shifting our focus toward artistic anatomy and constructive sculpting. Instead of stretching a single sphere, we will build a character by combining large primitive volumes and then carving them into clear, intentional planes.

Key Concepts

1. Professional Setup: Mirroring and Perspective

Before moving any clay, you must ensure your workspace is set for anatomical accuracy.

  • X-Symmetry: Always ensure X-Mirroring is enabled in the top right of your viewport. This maintains perfect balance and ensures your character's skeleton will function correctly for animation later.
  • Animator’s Focal Length: Standard camera views can warp a character's features. Open the Sidebar with N, go to the View tab, and set the Focal Length to a higher number like 100mm or 120mm. This provides a flatter, more natural view of the proportions.

2. Constructive Blocking: Primitive Strategy

In the animation industry, speed and structural integrity are key. Instead of "pulling" a torso out of a neck, we use a constructive approach by adding new geometry to act as large volumes.

  • Adding Volume: In Object Mode, use Shift + A to add Spheres or Subdivided Cubes to act as your primary masses.
  • Ribcage Blocking: Add a large sphere and use the Grab Tool to squash it into a large ovoid shape. This should be the largest volume in your bust.
  • Shoulder Placement: Add two smaller spheres tucked against the top of the ribcage to represent the shoulder girdle.
  • Joining and Fusing: Once your shapes are positioned, select them and your head, then press Ctrl + J to Join them. Use Ctrl + R to Voxel Remesh, which "fuses" these separate objects into a single, continuous skin of clay.

3. Resolution Management: Pre-Planar Prep

Before you start defining the "look" of your character, you must ensure your mesh density is appropriate for the larger surface area of a bust.

  • Visualizing Voxel Size: Press R to bring up the Voxel Size Grid. Because a torso is much larger than a head, you must adjust your density to ensure features like the collarbones don't look blocky.
  • Executing the Refresh: Press Ctrl + R to apply your resolution. You should do this every time you add a new primitive or make a massive structural change with the Grab Tool.

4. Planar Changes: Catching the Light

Once your resolution is set, you can begin the most important artistic step: defining Planar Changes. In animation, we avoid "round blobs" because they don't catch light effectively. We want flat surfaces that meet at clear angles.

  • Flatten Tool: This is your primary artistic tool this week. Use it to turn rounded areas of the chest, jaw, and brow into clean, flat planes.
  • Draw Tool: Use this to define "valleys" and "peaks," such as the hollow of the neck or the ridge of the collarbones.
  • Grab Tool: Use a large brush size (press F to resize) to adjust the overall "gesture" and silhouette of the planes.
  • Silhouette Check: Regularly toggle your view to see the model as a flat black shape to ensure the proportions are readable.

5. Artistic Landmarks: Head to Torso

Focus on these specific areas to bridge the head and body into a cohesive character:

  • Cranium and Jaw: These form the foundation of the head. Use the Flatten tool on the sides of the jaw to create a clean, stylized look.
  • Neck Bridge: Use the Draw tool to suggest the "V" shape of the neck muscles running from behind the ears to the center of the chest. Ensure the neck has a natural forward tilt.
  • Clavicles (Collarbones): These are the most important anchors. Use the Draw tool to carve the line from the shoulders to the sternum.
  • Pectoral Planes: Use the Flatten tool to turn the front of the ribcage into two distinct, flat chest planes.

Week 5 Assignment: Planar Personality

The goal of this assignment is to use planar changes to define character. I invite you to be creative with your designs; you are not limited to standard human proportions.

The Task: Create two distinct character blockouts that include a head and a torso. Focus entirely on using planar changes to define their personalities.

  • Character 1: Explore a design with wide, flat planes. You may optionally choose a heavy jawline or a sturdy chest to convey strength.
  • Character 2: Explore a design with sharp, narrow planes. You may optionally use an exaggerated, forward-tilted neck or high cheekbones to convey a more sinister or lithe personality.

Requirements:

  • Use the Constructive Blocking strategy (Spheres/Ovoids) for the ribcage and shoulders.
  • Show clear Planar Changes on the face and chest using the Flatten tool.
  • Submit: 3 screenshots (Front, Side, and Perspective) for each model. Use Orthographic views (Numpad 1, 3, 5) for your submission.
