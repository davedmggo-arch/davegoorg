Week 4: Basic Character: Head Sculpting

You are tasked with designing a basic character bust and transforming it from a high-detail sculpt into a functional, rigged asset capable of performing a simple animation.

Start Learning!!

Learning Outcomes

  • Master the sculpting interface and digital clay workflow to block out organic proportions.
  • Utilize Dynamic Topology and Multiresolution modifiers to manage mesh density during the sculpting process.
  • Identify and apply anatomical landmarks to create a structurally sound human head prototype.

Key Concepts

1. Sculpting Workflow: The "Clay" Mentality

Unlike the rigid box-modeling used for the table, sculpting allows you to push, pull, and smooth a high-density mesh as if it were physical clay.

  • Dynamic Topology (Dyntopo): This setting adds or removes geometry on the fly based on your brush strokes. It is ideal for the early stages of a sculpt because it allows you to change the shape of the mesh without pre-planning your geometry.
  • Multiresolution Modifier: This tool allows you to sculpt at different levels of detail non-destructively. You can work on the broad silhouette at a low resolution and "subdivide" to add fine details like wrinkles or skin pores later.

2. Head Modeling: Blocking the Form

Week 4 focuses strictly on proportions and silhouettes rather than fine details like eyelashes or nostrils.

  • The Primitive Start: Most character heads begin as a simple sphere or a subdivided cube.
  • Anatomy Blocking: Use the "Grab" and "Draw" brushes to define the primary volumes: the jawline, the brow ridge, the cranium, and the neck.
  • The Silhouette: Regularly turn off your materials and view the sculpt as a flat black shape against a white background to ensure the proportions are readable from every angle.

3. Sculpting Brushes & Tools

While Blender offers dozens of brushes, most of your work will be done with these core tools:

  • Grab Brush: Used for moving large areas of the mesh to adjust the overall shape and silhouette.
  • Draw/Clay Strips: Used to add "mass" to the model, perfect for building up muscle or bone structure.
  • Crease Brush: Used for carving sharp lines, such as the eyelids or the corners of the mouth.
  • Smooth Brush: Used to blend lumpy areas and soften transitions between forms.

4. Essential Shortcuts for Sculpting

  • Enter Sculpt Mode: Ctrl + Tab > Select Sculpt Mode from the pie menu.
  • Brush Size: Press F and drag to resize your brush.
  • Brush Strength: Press Shift + F to change the intensity of the stroke.
  • Smooth Shortcut: Hold Shift while using any active brush to temporarily switch to the Smooth brush.
  • Invert Brush: Hold Ctrl while sculpting to "push" the clay inward instead of pulling it out.

5. Best Practices for New Sculptors

  • Work Large to Small: Never add fine details until the overall "egg-shape" and jawline of the head are correct. Details cannot save a poor foundation.
  • Symmetry (X-Mirror): Ensure Symmetry is enabled on the X-axis so your changes are mirrored on both sides of the face automatically.
  • Use Reference: Keep a photo of a human skull or a reference sheet open. This helps ensure your ear and eye placement remains realistic.

6. Week 4 Assignment

  • The Task: Create a low-poly head prototype.
  • Step 1: Start with a sphere and block out the basic proportions of a head and neck.
  • Step 2: Use the Grab and Draw brushes to define the brow, nose bridge, and jaw.
  • Step 3: Ensure the silhouette looks correct from the front, side, and three-quarter views.
  • Submit: A screenshot of your sculpt from three different angles (Front, Side, and Perspective) showing the mesh structure.
