Project 2 Week 5

Week 4: Basic Character: Head Sculpting

You are tasked with designing a basic character bust and transforming it from a high-detail sculpt into a functional, rigged asset capable of performing a simple animation.

Start Learning!!

Learning Outcomes

  • Master the sculpting interface and digital clay workflow to block out organic proportions.
  • Utilize Dynamic Topology and Multiresolution modifiers to manage mesh density during the sculpting process.
  • Identify and apply anatomical landmarks to create a structurally sound human head prototype.

Key Concepts

1. Getting Started: The Sculpting Template

To skip the tedious setup, Blender provides a dedicated Sculpting File Template. Using this ensures your workspace is optimized for organic modeling from the moment you start.

  • Launch the Template: When opening Blender, select Sculpting from the "New File" splash screen.
  • The Quad Sphere: This template generates a "Quad Sphere." Unlike a standard UV sphere, it consists entirely of four-sided faces, which prevents "pinching" at the poles and provides a much cleaner surface for your brushes.
  • Optimized Workspace: The template automatically switches you to Sculpt Mode and enables "MatCap" lighting to make it easier to see the dips and bumps in your clay.

2. The Animator's Mindset: Appeal and Silhouette

In the animation industry, we don't just sculpt a human skull; we sculpt a character. An animator cares about how a character looks from a distance and how their face will move later.

  • The Silhouette Test: Regularly toggle your viewport to see your model as a flat black shape. If you can’t tell who the character is just from the outline, the forms aren't strong enough.
  • Flow Lines: Look for the "action lines" in the face. The curve of the jaw and the slope of the forehead should feel intentional and rhythmic.
  • Always Use Reference: Never sculpt from memory. Professional character designers always have a "model sheet" open. Use a tool like PureRef to keep front and side views of industry examples (like Disney or DreamWorks characters) on your screen at all times.

3. The Technical Core: Voxel Remeshing

We are using Voxel Remeshing to keep the clay feeling fresh. This allows you to push and pull the mesh as much as you want without the faces becoming "stretched" or broken. Do not use Dyntopo or Multiresolution for this stage.

  • Apply Scale First: Before you start, your object's scale must be 1.0. In Object Mode, press Ctrl + A and select Scale.
  • Visualize Voxel Size: Press Shift + R to bring up the Voxel Size Grid. A visual grid will appear over your mesh; use your mouse to adjust it to an appropriate density.
  • Execute Remesh: Once the grid looks right, press Ctrl + R to "refresh" the clay and apply the new resolution.

4. Blocking the Form with Symmetry

Before you move any clay, ensure X-Symmetry is enabled so your changes are mirrored on both sides of the face automatically.

The Animator's Lens (Focal Length)

By default, Blender’s camera is set to a 50mm focal length. When sculpting close up, this creates a "fish-eye" distortion that warps your character's features. To achieve a more natural look, press N to open the Sidebar, go to the View tab, and set the Focal Length to a higher number like 100mm or 120mm. This flattens the view and ensures your proportions are anatomically accurate as you work.

The Blocking Toolkit

  • Grab Brush (G): Your primary tool for moving large areas of the mesh to adjust the overall shape and silhouette.
  • Brush Size (F): Press F and drag to resize your brush.
  • Brush Strength (Shift + F): Press Shift + F to change the intensity of the stroke.
  • The Smooth Shortcut (Hold Shift): Hold Shift while using any active brush to temporarily switch to the Smooth brush and blend lumpy areas.

5a. Key Forms to Break Down

Success in character design comes from breaking the head down into large, manageable geometric shapes before diving into details. Use the following tools to achieve each primary form:

A. The Cranium (The Brain Case)

This is the foundation of your character's head. It should be large enough to house the brain and set the overall "weight" of the head.

  • Tool: Grab Brush (G)
  • How to use it: Use a very large brush size. From the side view (Numpad 3), pull the back of the sphere outward to create an elongated egg shape. From the front, ensure it tapers slightly toward the top.

5B. The Jaw and Chin

The jaw defines the character's personality and gender. In animation, this is often the most exaggerated feature.

  • Tool: Grab Brush (G) & Clay Strips
  • How to use it: Use the Grab brush to pull the bottom of the sphere downward and forward. To sharpen the "flat" planes of the jaw, use the Clay Strips brush to add mass along the jawline, then Hold Shift to smooth the edges into a clean plane.

5C. The Brow Ridge

Descriptio

The brow ridge is the primary landmark that separates the cranium from the face and dictates the character's "appeal" and expression.

  • Tool: Clay Strips or Draw Brush
  • How to use it: Draw a horizontal "shelf" across the middle of the face. Use Clay Strips to build up a thick ridge for a more masculine or heroic character, or the Draw brush for a softer, more subtle brow on younger or more appealing characters.

5D. The Neck

A poorly placed neck makes a character look robotic. It must tilt forward to feel "alive."

  • Tool: Grab Brush (G)
  • How to use it: Rotate to the side view (Numpad 3). Use a large Grab brush to pull a cylinder shape from the base of the skull. Immediately pull it slightly forward so it sits at an angle rather than dropping straight down.

5E. The Eye Sockets (The Mask Area)

Before adding eyes, you must create the "hollows" where they reside.

  • Tool: Draw Brush + Ctrl
  • How to use it: Position your brush just below the brow ridge. Hold Ctrl while drawing to push the clay inward, carving out two deep sockets. This creates the "mask" area common in animation character sheets.

6. The Trio Challenge: Diverse Blockouts

You are tasked with creating three distinct character head blockouts. Varying the primary volumes can instantly communicate different personalities.

  • The Heroic Archetype: Use a strong, square jawline and a prominent, sturdy brow ridge.
  • The Appealing Sidekick: Focus on circles and soft curves. Shorten the jaw and enlarge the cranium for a friendly, "squashy" silhouette.
  • The Exaggerated Antagonist: Push proportions to the extreme. Try a very long, narrow face, a sharp chin, or a predatory "hunch" in the neck.

7. Refining the "Chosen One": Adding Details

Choose your favorite blockout to take into the detailing phase. Move from "clay blocks" to recognizable features.

  • The Nose: Build a simple wedge-shaped bridge using the Clay Strips brush.
  • The Eye Sockets: Use the Draw brush while holding Ctrl to push into the mesh and create hollows.
  • The Ears: Block them out as simple "C" shapes using the Grab brush before adding internal folds.
  • The Mouth and Creases: Use the Crease brush to carve sharp lines for the eyelids and the lip line.

8. Managing Mesh Health: The Remesh Loop

When you pull the mesh significantly (like dragging a jaw down), the digital faces become stretched and "warped."

  • Spotting Warping: If your brush strokes look jagged or pixelated in one area, your mesh is warped.
  • The Fix: Continually perform a Voxel Remesh (Ctrl + R) after any major structural change to redistribute the geometry evenly.

9. Understanding Orthographic Views

To present your work professionally, you must use Orthographic Views to remove perspective distortion.

  • Front View: Press Numpad 1.
  • Side View: Press Numpad 3.
  • Perspective View: Rotate your view freely using the Middle Mouse Button.
  • Toggle Perspective/Ortho: Press Numpad 5.
