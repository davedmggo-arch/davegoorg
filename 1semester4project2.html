Project 2: Stylistic Dualism — Graphic vs. Realistic

Having established the world, we now create the life that lives within it. This project focuses on Character Expansion, moving from high-concept sculpting to technical execution and rigging.

Start Learning!!

Project Brief: Character Expansion

This project challenges you to take a single 2D character concept and translate it into two distinct 3D artistic directions. You will explore the "Style Spectrum" by creating one version of your character that is highly graphic (prioritizing 2D silhouette) and another that is more realistic (prioritizing believable anatomy). By the end of this project, you will have a deep understanding of how to manipulate form to achieve specific industry looks.

Part 1: The Modeling Phase

In this phase, we build the digital clay. The goal is to establish the silhouette and primary volumes of the character in a standard A-Pose to ensure they are ready for future rigging and animation.

  • Reference Integration: Drag and drop your 2D concept into the Blender viewport. Align your front and side views to the center of the world space and scale them appropriately.
  • Constructive Block-out: Using the "Lego" strategy, block out the head, neck, and torso using primitive spheres. In later sessions, you will add the arms and legs in a standard A-pose.
  • The Style Branch: Once your rough base mesh is complete, you will duplicate it. One mesh will be pushed toward Graphic Stylization (clean planes, bold shapes), while the other will be pushed toward Believable Realism (anatomical landmarks, soft transitions).
  • Accessories and Hair: After the body is blocked, you will sculpt hair and basic accessories. Hair should be treated as a "solid volume" rather than individual strands to maintain stylistic control.
  • Clothing: You will add clothing to both models. Remember that clothing should follow the style of the character—graphic clothing will have sharper, simplified folds, while realistic clothing will show more natural tension and weight.

Part 2: Retopology, Shaders, and Rendering

Once the modeling is finalized, we transition into the technical phase to prepare the characters for a professional presentation.

  • Retopology: You will create a clean, "low-poly" mesh over your high-density sculpts. This process ensures the geometry flows correctly around joints (like shoulders and knees) for clean deformation during animation.
  • Shaders and Color: Using the Shader Editor, you will apply color to your models. You may use a "Flat/Toon" shader for the graphic version and a more complex "PBR" (Physically Based Rendering) shader for the realistic version to emphasize the differences in style.
  • Lighting: You will set up a professional three-point lighting rig for each character. Lighting should be used to accentuate the planar changes in the graphic model and the subtle surface details of the realistic model.
  • Final Rendering: You will generate high-quality images of both characters from multiple angles to showcase your stylistic range.

Deliverables and Submission

At the end of the project, you are required to submit a professional package that demonstrates your mastery of both the artistic and technical workflows.

  • The Renders: High-resolution images of both the Graphic and Realistic characters (Front, Side, and 3/4 views).
  • The Blender File: A clean, organized .blend file containing both final models, your reference images, and your lighting setup.

Note: Throughout this project, keep your layers and collections organized. A professional animator's best friend is a clean file!
