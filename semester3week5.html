Week 5: Sculpting a Character

In this lesson, you’ll learn how to block out a character with primitives and then refine it using Blender 4.5’s most essential sculpting tools, focusing on forms before details. You’ll explore brushes, resolution management, and control tools like masking and symmetry to build a strong sculpting workflow from start to finish.

In-Class Topics:

  • Block out a character in an A-pose using primitives, the mirror modifier, and subdivision surface to establish clean, sculptable base forms.
  • Apply and manage voxel remeshing to unify geometry and control polygon density for different stages of sculpting.
  • Navigate Sculpt Mode efficiently using key UI panels, brush settings, and shortcuts for symmetry, brush control, and viewport shading.
  • Shape primary forms with core brushes such as Grab, Draw, Clay Strips, Smooth, Flatten, and Fill to establish silhouette and proportion.
  • Refine secondary forms with detail brushes such as Crease, Pinch, Inflate, and Scrape to enhance anatomy, folds, and medium features.
  • Apply industry design principles such as the 60/30/10 rule, silhouette readability, and asymmetry to create visually strong and believable characters.

Key Concepts & Tools:


Choosing the A-Pose

For this class, use the A-pose. The arms are angled slightly down, which keeps the shoulders relaxed and natural for sculpting. It also prepares the model for future deformation without extreme stretching in the joints. The A-pose is the most common starting stance for sculpting in modern pipelines.

Building the Blockout
  • Add primitives using Shift + A such as spheres for heads, cubes for torsos, and cylinders for limbs.
  • Arrange parts using Move, Rotate, and Scale until the proportions feel balanced.
  • Join parts into one object with Ctrl + J.
  • Add a Mirror Modifier in the Modifier Properties so both sides of the sculpt remain identical.
Subdivision Surface for Softer Forms
Add a Subdivision Surface modifier to your primitives before sculpting.
Set viewport levels to 2 or 3 for smooth base geometry.
Apply the modifier before switching into Sculpt Mode.
Subdivision creates a clay-like starting shape that responds more naturally to brushes.
Tips for the Blockout phase:
  • Always think about silhouette first. Check the model from multiple views to see if the character reads clearly.
  • Use as few primitives as possible in the blockout. Focus on the torso, limbs, and head before small details.
  • Start with low subdivision levels. Too much resolution too early encourages chasing detail before proportions are correct.
Remeshing for a Sculptable Base
Why Remesh Early
Blockouts often contain separate pieces and stretched geometry. Remesh rebuilds the surface into evenly distributed polygons that sculpt brushes can work on without distortion.

How to Use Remesh
  • Switch to Sculpt Mode.
  • Open the Remesh menu in the top header or the right Properties panel.
  • Set a voxel size between 0.01 and 0.02 meters for a human-sized character. This keeps the base around 20k to 40k polygons, which is good for primary sculpting.
  • Use Ctrl + R to remesh and rebuild topology.
  • Later, decrease voxel size to 0.005 to 0.008 meters for finer secondary forms such as facial features.

Tips:
  • Start sculpting only after remeshing. This ensures clean, predictable strokes.
  • Keep your voxel size larger at the beginning. Only remesh down when you are confident the proportions are correct.
  • Do not rush into fine detail until you have a stable and readable primary form.
Entering Sculpt Mode
Sculpt Mode Setup
  • Switch the object to Sculpt Mode from the top-left dropdown.
  • Enable X symmetry in the Symmetry panel so edits mirror across the model.
  • Change viewport shading to MatCap to better see form and volume.
  • Open the Brush Settings toolbar to adjust radius, strength, and falloff.

Key Shortcuts Before Brushes
  • F adjusts brush radius.
  • Shift + F adjusts brush strength.
  • Ctrl inverts a brush.
  • Shift smooths while sculpting.

Tips:
  • Set up your scene before sculpting. Correct symmetry, shading, and brush size shortcuts will save time.
  • Work zoomed out in early stages to focus on shape, not detail.
  • Remember that sculpting is additive. You will refine in passes, not all at once.
Primary Form Sculpting
Essential Brushes
  • Grab for moving large forms. (G)
  • Draw for building up shapes.(V)
  • Clay Strips for laying down volume. (C)
  • Smooth for blending surfaces. (S)

Workflow Advice
Work on the 60 percent big forms first. Establish torso, limbs, and head proportions before moving to details. Use large brush sizes at this stage to encourage broad edits instead of fine work.

Tips:
  • Check the silhouette often. A strong outline matters more than surface details.
  • Avoid zooming in too early. If it does not read from a distance, details will not fix it.
  • Spend the most time here. A strong foundation makes the rest of the sculpt easier.
Secondary Form Sculpting
Core Brushes
  • Crease to define folds and edges.
  • Pinch to tighten ridges and sharpen features.
  • Inflate and Deflate to push or reduce local volume.
  • Scrape and Peak to refine planes and highlight anatomy.

Workflow Advice
At this stage work on medium forms such as muscle groups, clothing folds, and facial features. Reduce brush size and strength for more control. Refine transitions between primary shapes without losing the larger silhouette.

Tips:
  • Use masking to protect areas while working on specific regions.
  • Refine proportions before locking in details.
  • Introduce asymmetry once secondary forms are in place for more realism.
Visual Development Strategy
60 / 30 / 10 Rule
  • 60 percent of sculpt should be large forms such as torso, limbs, and head.
  • 30 percent should be medium forms such as anatomy, folds, and secondary shapes.
  • 10 percent should be fine detail such as wrinkles, scars, and pores.

Lighting and Materials
  • Use different MatCaps and rotate lighting angles to check how forms read.
  • Work with rim lighting or high contrast shaders to test silhouette clarity.

Tips:
  • Detail with intent. Wrinkles and surface marks should tell a story about the character.
  • Strong design is more important than surface noise.
  • Avoid over detailing early. Simplicity at the right stage reads stronger than scattered detail.
Wrap-Up and Polishing
Final Pass
  • Review silhouette from multiple views.
  • Evaluate proportions against reference images.
  • Add subtle asymmetry to finalize realism.

Industry Tips:
  • A sculpt that reads clearly at a distance is stronger than one packed with unnecessary micro detail.
  • Keep iteration flexible. Do not hesitate to remesh and simplify if proportions drift.
  • Always step away and check the sculpt with fresh eyes before calling it finished.
