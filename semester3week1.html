What You'll Learn

  • Getting comfortable with Blender's interface and how to customize it for your needs
  • Learning how to move around in 3D space (it's easier than it looks!)
  • Creating and manipulating basic 3D shapes
  • Understanding the 3D coordinate system (X, Y, Z axes)
  • Introduction to using reference materials (funpacks) for your projects

Key Concepts & Tools


Interface Fundamentals:
  • Understanding Blender's workspace layout: Viewport, Outliner, Properties, Timeline
  • How to organize and customize panels to suit your workflow
  • Different ways to view your work (Solid, Wireframe, Material Preview, Rendered)
  • Switching between different workspaces (Modeling, Sculpting, Animation)
Panel Organization
  • Viewport: Your main window into the 3D world where you'll create and interact with objects. It's important because this is where you'll spend most of your time visualizing your work from different angles and perspectives.
  • Outliner: A hierarchical list showing all objects, collections, and data in your scene. This is crucial for organizing complex scenes, selecting specific objects when they overlap, and managing the structure of your project efficiently.
  • Properties Panel: Where you adjust settings for selected objects, scenes, or tools. It's divided into tabs (like the wrench for modifiers, material sphere for textures, etc.). Understanding this panel is essential because it gives you precise control over every aspect of your scene.
  • Timeline: The animation control panel showing frames and playback controls. Even if you're not animating yet, it's important to understand this panel as it controls temporal aspects of Blender and will be fundamental in future animation work.
  • Panel Organization and Customization: Blender allows you to rearrange, resize, split, and join panels by dragging their corners. This is important because customizing your workspace to match your workflow significantly improves efficiency and reduces time searching for tools.
Viewport Shading Modes
Solid: Shows objects with basic shading and colors. Important for quickly assessing form and volume without distractions.
Wireframe: Displays only the edges of your models. Crucial for examining topology and structure during modeling.
Material Preview: Shows how materials will look with lighting and textures. Important for getting a realistic preview while still maintaining fast performance.
Rendered: Uses the render engine to show near-final results. Important for checking lighting, materials, and overall scene appearance before final rendering.
Navigation Controls
Middle Mouse Button (MMB): Rotate your view around objects
Shift + MMB: Move your view (panning)
Ctrl + MMB or Mouse Wheel: Zoom in and out
Numpad 1: Front view
Numpad 3: Right side view
Numpad 7: Top view
Numpad 5: Switch between orthographic and perspective views
Numpad 0: See through your camera's lens
Numpad . (period): Focus on what you've selected
Object Manipulation
G: Grab/Move objects
R: Rotate objects
S: Scale objects (make them bigger or smaller)
X, Y, Z: Constrain movement to a specific axis (press twice for local axis)
Shift + X, Y, Z: Move along all axes except the one you selected
Ctrl + Z: Undo (you'll use this a lot!)
Shift + Ctrl + Z: Redo
Object Management
A: Select all objects
Alt + A: Deselect everything
Shift + A: Add new objects
X: Delete selected objects
Shift + D: Duplicate objects
H: Hide selected objects (useful when things get crowded)
Alt + H: Unhide all objects
M: Move objects to different collections (like folders)
Basic Modeling Tools
Tab: Switch between Edit and Object mode
1, 2, 3: Switch between Vertex, Edge, and Face selection modes
Ctrl + Tab: Quick access to mode selection
Alt + G: Reset an object's position
Alt + R: Reset an object's rotation
Alt + S: Reset an object's scale
Tips and Tricks
  • The Three-Click Navigation Rule: Remember this: MMB to rotate, Shift+MMB to pan, and Ctrl+MMB to zoom. This simple pattern will become your best friend for navigating 3D space efficiently.
  • Numpad Navigation is Faster: While the viewport gizmo looks tempting, using the numpad for standard views (1, 3, 7) is much quicker once you get used to it. It's a professional habit worth developing early.
  • Watch Your Object's Origin: That little orange dot represents your object's center point. When transforming objects, always check where this origin is located. If it's in the wrong place, use Ctrl+Shift+Alt+C to fix it.
  • Save Versions, Not Just Files: Instead of overwriting your work, use Ctrl+Alt+S to save new versions. This creates a safety net and lets you compare your progress over time. Future you will thank present you!
Why Saving Matters:
In 3D work, saving isn't just about preserving your fileâ€”it's about creating a safety net and enabling iterative development. Losing hours of work due to a crash or oversight is frustrating and avoidable. Good saving habits also allow you to experiment freely and revert to earlier versions if needed.

Key Saving Concepts:
Save vs. Save As
  • Save (Ctrl + S): Overwrites the current file with your latest changes. Use this frequently for small incremental saves.
  • Save As (Shift + Ctrl + S): Creates a new file, leaving the original intact. Use this when you reach a milestone or want to try a new direction without losing your current work.
Incremental Saving
  • Instead of just overwriting, save versions with numbers (e.g., project_v01.blend, project_v02.blend). This creates a history of your project and allows you to go back if something goes wrong. It's especially important before major changes like applying modifiers or complex operations.

Homework

  • Build Your First Table: Create a simple table using only basic shapes (cubes, cylinders) and transformations. Focus on making it look proportional and well-aligned.
