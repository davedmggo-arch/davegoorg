Week 8: The Blocking Phase

In this first stage, you will move from a broad idea to three distinct 3D visual concepts by exploring silhouettes and primary forms. Your goal is to establish a strong visual foundation that communicates Smyder's "nefarious" personality before you commit to a final design.

The Prop Options: Recap

  • The Static-Claw Gauntlet (Hands): A heavy, copper-wired leather glove used to generate static electricity. It’s perfect for scrambling Wi-Fi and making the islanders' clothes stick together.
  • The Feline-Sight Visor (Eyes/Head): A multi-lensed headset with metal cat ears. It gives you the ability to see through walls to find hidden tuna and secret yarn stashes.
  • The Sonic-Hiss Chestpiece (Body): A mechanical chest plate with a brass speaker shaped like a roaring cat. It emits a vibration that scares off dogs and melts ice cream instantly.

1. The Static-Claw Gauntlet

Search Terms: Steampunk gauntlet, copper induction coils, falconry glove, cat claws macro, static generator.
Advice: Focus on the "power source." Does the electricity come from a battery on the wrist, or is it generated by the friction of the claws? When blocking out the glove, make the fingers look slightly oversized and aggressive to emphasize the "claw" aspect.

2. The Feline-Sight Visor

Search Terms: Vintage night vision goggles, cyberpunk eyewear, cat ear silhouette, brass telescope, multi-lens camera.
Advice: The "eyes" are the most important part here. Try blocking out different sized cylinders for the lenses. Some can be small and precise, while others are large and bulky. Make sure the metal "cat ears" on top look like they are part of the antenna system.

3. The Sonic-Hiss Chestpiece

Search Terms: Gramophone horn, vintage diving bell, mechanical breastplate, bellows, cat roar anatomy.
Advice: The focal point is the speaker. Start by blocking out a large cone or "bell" shape in the center of the chest. Use rounded boxes for the air canisters. Think about how the straps would wrap around your shoulders to support the weight of the heavy brass.

Creating Your Variations

Once you have chosen your prop, you need to create three different "roughs" in 3D. This is not about being perfect; it is about exploration.

  • Variation A (The "Heavy" Look): Use large, chunky blocks. Make the prop look industrial, weighted, and intimidating.

  • Variation B (The "Scrappy" Look): Use many smaller, thin shapes. Make it look like you and your cats built it from junk found on the beach.

  • Variation C (The "Sleek" Look): Use long, curved, and aerodynamic shapes. This version should look like high-tech "cat-standard" equipment.

Compare the three side-by-side. Which one looks most like something a mischievous boy would wear? Which one has the coolest silhouette?

Working with Color

At this stage, you are only using "Block Colors" to define the different materials. Do not worry about textures yet.

  • The "Nefarious" Palette: Use dark, moody colors like deep purples, charcoal grays, or forest greens.

  • The Contrast: Add one or two "high-energy" colors for the functional parts. Think glowing neon green for the visor lenses or bright copper for the gauntlet wires.

  • Visual Balance: Use the 60-30-10 rule. 60% should be your primary dark color, 30% a secondary metal or leather color, and 10% should be your bright "evil" accent color.

  • Reference Existing Colors: The character utilizing the props should be considered when designing your props! Use the language of your character to and suppliment them with your own.
